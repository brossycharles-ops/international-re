Legal Guide — Panama

Can Foreigners Buy Property in Panama?
2026 Complete Legal Guide

By Carlos Rivera Updated April 14, 2026 10 min read

Panama offers one of the most foreigner-friendly property ownership regimes in all of Latin America. As a foreign buyer, you have nearly identical rights to Panamanian citizens — no special permits, no government approval, and no residency requirement to purchase. Here is everything you need to know before signing anything.

Ownership Rights: What Foreigners Can (and Cannot) Buy

Panama's constitution grants foreign nationals the same real property rights as citizens for almost all residential and commercial real estate. You can own titled property outright in your personal name, through a Panamanian corporation, or through a foundation — and there is no mandatory escrow or joint-ownership requirement.

Two constitutional restrictions apply specifically to foreigners:

  • Border exclusion zone: Foreign nationals cannot purchase land within 10 kilometers of Panama's international borders with Costa Rica or Colombia. This primarily affects rural and beachfront parcels in Chiriqui (near Costa Rica) and Darién (near Colombia). Always confirm a property's distance from the border before making an offer in these regions.
  • Indigenous comarcas: Land within Panama's autonomous indigenous territories (comarcas) is collectively owned and cannot be titled or sold to any private buyer, foreign or national.
Key Takeaway

Outside these two zones, Panama is one of the easiest countries in Latin America for foreigners to own real estate outright. No residency, no visa, no government approval — just a valid passport and a notary.

Titled Property vs. Rights of Possession

Not all land in Panama carries the same legal protections. Understanding the difference between the two main property types is essential before you buy:

Titled Property (Finca)

A titled property — called a finca — is registered in the Panama Public Registry and carries a unique folio number. This is the gold standard of Panamanian real estate. Titled properties are the easiest to finance, insure, resell, and use to qualify for residency visas. Always insist on a title search (estudio de título) before purchase to verify there are no liens, encumbrances, or ownership disputes.

Rights of Possession (Derecho Posesorio)

Some rural and coastal land in Panama — particularly older beach plots — is held as derecho posesorio (rights of possession). These are informal ownership claims that have not yet been formally titled. They can be converted to titled property through a process called adjudication, but this takes time, legal work, and is not guaranteed. Rights of possession carry significantly higher risk for foreign buyers and cannot typically be used to qualify for investment-based residency. Avoid them unless you are working with a highly experienced local attorney who specializes in this process.

The Buying Process: Step by Step

A typical Panama property purchase takes 30 to 60 days from accepted offer to registered title. The process is handled entirely through private parties — there is no government purchase committee or foreign-buyer approval board.

  1. Hire a Panamanian real estate attorney. Unlike in some countries, using an attorney in Panama is both customary and strongly advised. Your attorney will run the title search, draft contracts, and coordinate the closing. Legal fees typically run 1–1.5% of the purchase price.

  2. Sign a promise-to-buy agreement (promesa de compraventa). This bilateral contract locks in the price, terms, and closing date. A good-faith deposit of 5–10% is standard. Make sure your contract specifies what happens to the deposit if the deal falls through.

  3. Conduct due diligence. Your attorney will search the Public Registry for liens, verify that property taxes are current, confirm the seller's legal standing, and check for any rights-of-possession claims on adjoining land.

  4. Execute the public deed (escritura pública). The formal transfer deed must be drafted and authenticated by a Panamanian notary public. Both buyer and seller (or their legal representatives) must sign before the notary.

  5. Register the transfer at the Public Registry. The signed deed is presented to the Public Registry (Registro Público) to record the change of ownership. This step makes the transfer legally effective against third parties. Registry fees are paid at this stage.

Important

Wire all purchase funds to your attorney's trust account — never directly to a seller. Panama has strong anti-money-laundering regulations and your attorney is required to verify the source of funds. Budget extra time for bank compliance checks if transferring large sums internationally.

Closing Costs Breakdown

Panama's closing costs are moderate by regional standards. Buyer-side costs typically run 3–4% of the purchase price, while the seller bears the largest cost — the transfer tax. Total round-trip transaction costs (buy + eventual sell) run approximately 7–9%.

Law 468 — New-Build Tax Change

Panama's Law 468 (April 24, 2025) eliminated the historic transfer tax exemption for first sales of new residential properties, effective January 1, 2026. If you are buying a new-build, budget for the full 2% transfer tax — the seller-side exemption no longer applies. Some developers may absorb this cost in negotiations; always confirm in writing.

Annual Property Taxes

Panama taxes real property based on its registered cadastral value — typically lower than market value — using a progressive scale. Rates differ depending on whether the property is your primary residence or a secondary/investment property.

Primary Residence (Tributary Family Patrimony)

If you designate a property as your primary residence (Patrimonio Familiar Tributario), you benefit from a full exemption on the first US$120,000 of registered value:

Residency Pathways Through Real Estate

Panama offers multiple routes to permanent residency, several of which are tied to property ownership. These are among the most accessible in the region.

1. Friendly Nations Visa

Citizens of roughly 50 "friendly nations" — including the United States, Canada, the UK, Australia, all EU countries, and most of Latin America — can apply for Panamanian residency by demonstrating economic ties. Real estate investment is one of the qualifying methods:

  • Minimum investment: US$200,000 in titled Panamanian real estate (can be financed through a Panamanian bank)
  • Timeline: ~2-year provisional residency, then eligible for permanent residency
  • No minimum stay requirement during the provisional period

2. Qualified Investor Visa

For buyers making a larger investment, Panama's Qualified Investor Visa offers a more direct path:

  • Investments made on or before October 15, 2026: minimum US$300,000
  • Investments made after October 16, 2026: minimum US$500,000
  • Grants permanent residency directly, without a provisional period
Deadline Alert

The lower US$300,000 threshold for the Qualified Investor Visa expires October 15, 2026. Buyers who want to lock in the lower investment requirement should begin the legal process well in advance of that date. The threshold rises to US$500,000 the following day.

3. Pensionado (Retiree) Visa

Panama's Pensionado program is widely considered the best retiree visa in the Americas. Requirements:

  • Option A: Lifetime pension income of at least US$1,000/month (Social Security, government pension, or approved private pension)
  • Option B: US$750/month pension plus a US$100,000 property purchase in Panama
  • Grants immediate permanent residency — no provisional period
  • Holders receive significant discounts: 20–50% on healthcare, 25% on utility bills, 25% on airline tickets, and more

Practical Checklist for Foreign Buyers

  • ✓  Hire a bilingual Panamanian real estate attorney before making any offer
  • ✓  Confirm the property is titled (finca) — not derecho posesorio
  • ✓  Run a full title search through the Public Registry
  • ✓  Verify property tax payments are current and there are no outstanding liens
  • ✓  For border-area properties: confirm distance from the Costa Rica / Colombia border
  • ✓  Wire purchase funds through your attorney's trust account, not directly to the seller
  • ✓  Budget 3–4% of purchase price for buyer-side closing costs
  • ✓  If using property for residency: confirm the investment threshold that applies on your purchase date
Bottom Line

Panama is one of the most legally straightforward places in Latin America to buy real estate as a foreigner. The dollar-denominated economy, clear title system, and multiple residency pathways make it a standout for both lifestyle buyers and investors. Work with a licensed Panamanian attorney, stick to titled property, and verify your closing costs in writing — and the process is remarkably smooth.
