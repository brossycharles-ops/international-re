Section 01

Executive Summary — Why Latin America, Why Now

Latin America is quietly becoming the world's most compelling region for foreign real estate investment. A combination of dollar-denominated pricing, rising tourism, and favorable legal frameworks for foreigners is drawing a new wave of buyers from North America and Europe.

In 2026, four countries stand out above all others for foreign buyers: Costa Rica, Nicaragua, Argentina, and Chile. Each offers a distinct risk/reward profile, and together they represent a comprehensive spectrum — from the mature, infrastructure-rich markets of Costa Rica and Chile to the emerging, high-upside opportunities of Nicaragua and Argentina.

The Macro Case

Several structural forces are converging to make this one of the most attractive entry windows in recent memory:

  • Dollar strength: With most Latin American markets pricing in or indexing to USD, American buyers face minimal currency conversion disadvantage while benefiting from local labor and operating costs denominated in weakened local currencies.
  • Remote work normalization: The post-pandemic shift to location independence created a permanent, high-income tenant class across all four markets — particularly in beach and mountain destinations.
  • Infrastructure investment: All four countries are in active phases of road, airport, and tourism infrastructure development, historically preceding real estate appreciation cycles of 15–30% over 5-year periods.
  • Foreign buyer rights: Unlike Southeast Asia or parts of Eastern Europe, all four countries offer foreigners near-identical property rights to citizens — freehold ownership, proper title registration, and legal mortgage access.
  • Tourism tailwinds: International arrivals to the region grew 18% year-over-year in 2025, with projections of further 12–15% growth through 2026, directly supporting short-term rental yields.

◆ Key Insight

Foreign buyers who entered these four markets between 2018 and 2020 saw average appreciation of 22–38% by 2025, depending on location and market segment. The 2026 window is supported by a similar convergence of tourism growth, currency dynamics, and infrastructure spending.

How to Use This Guide

Each market section covers average price per square meter by region, rental yield data from operating property managers, the legal process for foreign buyers (including timelines and costs), tax implications, and our investment recommendation for different buyer profiles. The guide closes with a universal buying checklist and a comparison table to help you prioritize your target market.

We recommend reading the full guide even if you have a preferred market in mind — comparative context often reveals the strongest entry point is not the one you initially expected.

Costa Rica is the benchmark for foreign real estate investment in Latin America. With a stable democracy, dollarized real estate market, strong rule of law, and world-class tourism infrastructure, it offers the lowest risk profile of the four countries in this guide — at a premium price.

Market Overview

The Costa Rican real estate market had a notable correction of approximately 28–31% from its 2023 peak pricing in certain coastal zones, largely driven by interest rate pressures in buyer home countries. This correction has since stabilized, and Q1 2026 data shows appreciation resuming at 5–7% annually across core investment corridors.

Foreign buyer activity accounted for approximately 38% of all residential transactions in 2025, a record high, with North American buyers representing 71% of that cohort. The market is well-serviced by English-speaking attorneys, bilingual property managers, and established title insurance providers.

Best Areas for Investment

Guanacaste (Tamarindo, Nosara, Flamingo) remains the top-performing investment region due to direct international flights into Liberia Airport (LIR), high tourist density, and the active short-term rental market. Q1 2026 data showed 29% increased rental demand against 8% decreased available inventory — a classic supply squeeze.

The Southern Zone (Uvita, Ojochal, Dominical) is the emerging play for value-oriented buyers. Prices remain 40–60% below comparable Guanacaste properties, the scenery is arguably superior, and infrastructure development is 3–5 years behind, suggesting a meaningful appreciation window.

The Central Valley (Escazu, Santa Ana) suits buyers seeking long-term residential tenants rather than vacation rental income. Corporate relocation demand, a large expat community, and proximity to San Jose's international airport support consistent 4.5–6.5% gross yields with lower management intensity.

Legal Process for Foreigners

Costa Rica's constitutional protections give foreigners identical property rights to citizens. Foreign buyers can own property as individuals or through a Costa Rican corporation (Sociedad Anonima), which is often recommended for liability management and estate planning.

  1. Hire a qualified attorney — non-negotiable. Legal fees typically run 1–1.5% of purchase price.
  2. Title search and due diligence — attorney searches the National Registry for liens, encumbrances, and boundary disputes. Takes 2–4 weeks.
  3. Preliminary purchase agreement (Opcion de Compra) — sets price, deposit (typically 10%), and closing timeline.
  4. Closing via Notary Public — Costa Rica uses notaries (who must be licensed attorneys) rather than escrow companies for closings. All transfers are registered in the National Registry.
  5. Transfer taxes and closing costs — transfer tax is 1.5% of the registered value, plus stamps and registration fees totaling approximately 0.5–0.7%. Total closing costs for buyers typically run 2.5–3.5% of purchase price.

Timeline

From signed purchase agreement to recorded title: typically 45–90 days. Transactions involving rural or coastal properties may take longer due to concession status verification (see below).

⚠ Maritime Zone Law — Critical for Beach Properties

Properties within 200 meters of the high-tide line fall under the Maritime Terrestrial Zone Law. The first 50 meters (Public Zone) cannot be privately owned by anyone. The remaining 150 meters (Restricted Zone) can only be held under a concession — not freehold title. Concessions carry lease-like terms and are not renewable by foreigners who have not been legal residents for 5+ years. Always verify maritime zone status before purchasing coastal property.

Tax Implications

  • Annual property tax: 0.25% of registered value (municipalities assess annually).
  • Luxury home tax (Impuesto Solidario): Properties registered above ~$270,000 are subject to a progressive luxury tax of 0.25%–0.55% of assessed value.
  • Rental income tax: 15% withholding on gross rental income for non-resident owners.
  • Capital gains tax: 15% on gains from property sales (introduced 2019), with deductions available for improvements and inflation adjustments.
  • No inheritance tax in Costa Rica.

🇳🇮 Nicaragua

Central America's Best Value — High Upside, Elevated Risk, Low Entry Point

$650 Avg $/sqm
8.5% Avg Yield
Medium Legal Risk

Nicaragua is Central America's most price-accessible real estate market and arguably its most misunderstood. Political tensions of 2018–2022 created a buyer's exodus that decimated prices — and that same period may prove to be the buying opportunity of a generation for investors with appropriate risk tolerance.

Market Overview

Nicaragua's property market bottomed in 2020–2021 and has been in a measured recovery since. International buyer activity, while below pre-2018 levels, has been increasing steadily — driven primarily by budget-conscious retirees, expat entrepreneurs, and value investors who recognize the fundamental appeal: Pacific beach access, colonial city charm, and prices that remain 50–70% below comparable Costa Rican properties.

The rental market is bifurcated: a strong long-term tenant market (primarily expats and NGO/diplomatic workers in Managua) and a recovering short-term vacation market centered on San Juan del Sur and Granada. Both are showing positive momentum in 2026.

Emerging Areas to Watch

The Pacific Corridor (Poneloya to Masachapa) offers the highest upside among Nicaragua's emerging zones. These Pacific beaches are within 90–120 minutes of Managua's international airport and remain almost entirely undiscovered by foreign buyers. Land values in 2026 range from $15–$45/sqm — representing a potential 3–5x appreciation event if the region follows San Juan del Sur's development trajectory over the next decade.

Ometepe Island — an ecological reserve on Lake Nicaragua — attracts eco-tourism investors. Regulations limit development density, which constrains supply and supports premium pricing for compliant eco-lodges and retreat properties.

Legal Process for Foreigners

Nicaragua's constitution guarantees foreigners the same property rights as citizens. Freehold ownership is available without residency requirements. The legal process closely parallels Costa Rica's but with some important distinctions:

  1. Hire an attorney experienced with foreign transactions — fees typically 1–1.5% of purchase price. English-speaking attorneys are concentrated in San Juan del Sur, Granada, and Managua.
  2. Title verification — critically important. Nicaragua's land registry has historical gaps, and some properties have competing claims stemming from the 1980s Sandinista-era expropriations. Your attorney must conduct an exhaustive title search going back at least 30 years.
  3. Purchase agreement and deposit — typically 10% held in an attorney's trust account.
  4. Closing via Notary — public notary registers the transfer at the Registry of Property.
  5. Closing costs — transfer tax 1%, notary/legal fees 1–1.5%, registration fees 0.5–1%. Total buyer costs: approximately 3–4% of purchase price.

⚠ Risks to Understand Before Buying

  • Title risk: Historic expropriations and informal land transactions mean title disputes are more common than in Costa Rica. Title insurance is available through Nicaraguan providers and is strongly recommended.
  • Political risk: The regulatory environment remains subject to government policy shifts. Diversification and careful entity structuring can mitigate (but not eliminate) this risk.
  • Liquidity risk: The buyer pool is thinner than Costa Rica, meaning exit timelines may be longer in a forced-sale scenario.
  • Infrastructure gap: Power, water, and road infrastructure outside major population centers and established tourist zones is inconsistent.

Tax Implications

  • Annual property tax (IBI): 1% of cadastral value (which is typically below market value).
  • Rental income tax: 10–15% of net rental income for non-residents.
  • Capital gains tax: 10% on property sale profits.
  • Transfer tax at purchase: 1% of sale price.
  • Nicaragua offers various investment incentive programs (Law 306 for tourism projects) that can provide multi-year tax exemptions for qualifying developments.

🇦🇷 Argentina

The USD Advantage Play — World-Class City Assets at Emerging Market Prices

$1,800 BA Avg $/sqm
6.5% Avg Yield
Medium Legal Risk

Argentina's property market in 2026 is a tale of extraordinary opportunity inside an economy undergoing painful but necessary structural reform. Buenos Aires real estate, priced in USD, sits at valuations last seen in the early 2000s — offering buyers world-class European-style architecture and urban amenities at prices that seem almost impossible given the quality on offer.

Market Overview

Argentina's currency crisis of 2019–2024 devastated local purchasing power but, paradoxically, created one of the world's most attractive entry points for dollar-holding foreign buyers. Buenos Aires property is almost universally transacted in USD — this is not a formality, it is an entrenched market norm. A two-bedroom apartment in Palermo that sold for $180,000 in 2017 may trade for $130,000–$145,000 in 2026, despite Argentine inflation running at multi-hundred-percent annually in peso terms.

President Milei's market liberalization agenda, underway since late 2023, has begun stabilizing the macro environment. The informal dollar exchange rate (which drove an 80–100% premium over official rates for several years) has largely converged with the official rate — a critical normalization that simplifies transactions for foreign buyers and signals improving institutional confidence.

The USD Advantage Explained

For foreign buyers, Argentina's informal dual-currency economy creates a structural advantage that no other market in this guide can replicate. When bringing USD into Argentina and converting through legitimate financial channels, dollar holders access Buenos Aires real estate at what amounts to a persistent discount to its fundamental value — because local purchasing power is denominated in a perpetually inflating currency.

The Airbnb and short-term rental market compounds this advantage: listing in USD or EUR while operating costs (utilities, maintenance, property management) are paid in pesos creates an operating margin structure that simply does not exist in other markets.

Rental Yields and the Short-Term Market

Buenos Aires has become one of the most popular cities globally for digital nomads, and Airbnb occupancy in Palermo and San Telmo regularly exceeds 65–75% for well-positioned units. Nightly rates for quality one-bedroom apartments range from $60–$120, with premium units in Recoleta and Palermo commanding $120–$200.

Gross short-term rental yields in top neighborhoods average 7–10%. After management fees (typically 20–25% for full-service management) and peso-denominated operating costs, net yields of 5–7% are realistic and represent exceptional risk-adjusted returns given the asset quality on offer.

Legal Process for Foreigners

Argentina's legal framework for foreign real estate ownership is well-established. Foreigners can own property directly as individuals without requiring residency or a local partner.

  1. Obtain a CDI (Clave de Identificacion) — the equivalent of a tax ID for foreigners. Obtained at AFIP (Argentine tax authority) offices; takes 1–3 days.
  2. Open a local bank account — required for formal property transactions, though some transactions are structured as cash (USD) exchanges. Your attorney will advise based on current regulations.
  3. Hire a Buenos Aires real estate attorney (Escribano) — Argentina uses public notaries called Escribanos for all property closings. Legal/notary fees: approximately 1.5–2% of purchase price.
  4. Sign Boleto de Compraventa (Purchase Contract) — typically with 30% deposit.
  5. Closing (Escritura) — the Escribano formalizes the transfer and registers with the Property Registry. Process from contract to closing: 30–60 days.
  6. Transfer taxes and costs — seller typically pays a 1.5–3% transfer tax; buyer pays notary fees and registry costs. Total buyer closing costs: 2–3% of purchase price.

◆ Argentina Investment Profile Summary

Best for: Buyers seeking world-class urban real estate at emerging market prices, investors comfortable with macro uncertainty, short-term rental operators.

Not ideal for: Risk-averse buyers, those requiring easy capital repatriation, buyers with short investment horizons (under 3 years).

Watch carefully: Capital controls on repatriation of proceeds — regulations have shifted frequently. Engage a tax attorney in both your home country and Argentina before committing.

Tax Implications

  • Annual property tax (ABL in Buenos Aires): Low by global standards — typically $200–$800/year for a standard apartment.
  • Bienes Personales (wealth tax): Non-resident foreigners owning Argentine property are subject to wealth tax on property value at 0.5–1.75% depending on value tier.
  • Rental income tax: Non-residents pay 24.5% on gross rental income (12.5% net after the 50% deduction allowed by Argentine tax law for foreign renters).
  • Capital gains: Transfers held less than 2 years are subject to a 15% capital gains tax on profit. Properties held longer benefit from an inflation adjustment mechanism.

🇨🇱 Chile

South America's Most Stable Economy — Institutional Quality, Premium Pricing

$2,400 Santiago $/sqm
5.5% Avg Yield
Low Legal Risk

Chile is the region's most institutionally sophisticated real estate market. With OECD membership, a peso that has historically maintained relative stability, a transparent legal system, and a growing upper-middle class, Chile offers the closest analog to a developed-market investment experience in Latin America.

Market Overview

The Chilean property market in 2026 is characterized by steady demand in Santiago — particularly in the upmarket eastern communes — against a constrained supply pipeline caused by higher construction costs and tightened bank lending criteria since 2022. This supply-demand imbalance has supported price appreciation of 4–6% annually in Santiago's prime zones despite a slowing broader economy.

Coastal and southern markets — Valparaiso, the Lake District, and Patagonia-adjacent areas — are experiencing a different dynamic: a surge in lifestyle-driven foreign buyers and domestic second-home buyers, supported by improving air connectivity and a growing premium tourism sector.

Santiago — The Investment Case

Santiago's Las Condes, Vitacura, and Lo Barnechea communes form one of Latin America's most resilient real estate markets. Populated by South America's strongest corporate sector, a large diplomatic community, and Chile's wealthy upper class, prime Santiago real estate has appreciated in USD terms in 18 of the last 22 years — a track record unmatched anywhere else in the region.

The Nunoa and Providencia communes offer value relative to the eastern premium zones. These dense, walkable urban neighborhoods with excellent metro access attract younger professional tenants and expat long-term renters, supporting consistent 5–6.5% gross yields on well-located apartments.

Valparaiso & the Coast

Valparaiso's historic hillside neighborhoods (cerros) are a UNESCO World Heritage Site and a growing destination for cultural tourism and lifestyle buyers. Properties in the upper cerros with ocean views are commanding increasing premiums. Vina del Mar, immediately adjacent, offers a more conventional beach resort market with established demand from Santiago weekenders.

The Lake District — Chile's Lifestyle Play

The Lake District around Pucon and Puerto Varas is Chile's most compelling lifestyle destination for foreign buyers in 2026. Set against the backdrop of snow-capped volcanoes and pristine glacial lakes, the region offers a quality of natural scenery that rivals Patagonia at a fraction of the entry price.

Pucon in particular has developed a mature short-term rental market driven by outdoor adventure tourism (skiing, white-water rafting, trekking) with year-round demand. Gross yields of 6–9% on well-managed lodge or cabin properties are supported by 70–80% average occupancy through both summer and winter high seasons.

Legal Process for Foreigners

Chile has one of Latin America's most transparent and straightforward property ownership frameworks for foreigners.

  1. Obtain a RUT (Rol Unico Tributario) — Chile's universal tax ID, required for all property transactions. Obtained at any SII (Internal Revenue Service) office or online. Can be processed in 1–2 days.
  2. Hire a Chilean attorney (Abogado) — legal fees 0.5–1% of purchase price. Chile's legal system is well-regarded and English-speaking attorneys are available in Santiago and major tourist areas.
  3. Sign Promesa de Compraventa (Purchase Promise) — with deposit of 10–20%. Fully binding and notarized.
  4. Mortgage (optional) — Chilean banks will lend to foreigners with demonstrated income, typically up to 60–70% LTV. Rates in 2026 range from 5.5–7.5% fixed.
  5. Closing via Notary (Notaria) — both parties sign before a notary. The Conservador de Bienes Raices (Property Registry) registers the transfer, typically within 2–5 business days of signing.
  6. Closing costs — notary fees 0.3–0.5% of purchase price, Property Registry 0.2–0.4%, title study 0.1–0.2%. Total buyer closing costs: approximately 1–1.5%.

✓ Chile Advantage — Lowest Closing Costs in the Region

Chile's total buyer closing costs of 1–1.5% are the lowest of the four markets in this guide, and the legal process is the most standardized. For buyers prioritizing legal certainty and institutional quality, Chile frequently ranks first despite its higher per-sqm pricing.

Tax Implications

  • Annual property tax (Contribuciones): 1.2% of fiscal assessed value (typically 70–80% of market value) for properties above the exempt threshold (~UF 40,000 / approx. $55,000). First-home exemptions do not apply to foreigners.
  • Rental income tax: 35% withholding on gross income for non-resident foreigners (reduced by treaties with certain countries).
  • Capital gains tax: Properties held more than 4 years are exempt from capital gains tax up to UF 8,000 (~$11,000) of gain per year. Gains above this threshold are taxed at 10%.
  • VAT on new construction: New properties purchased from developers are subject to 19% VAT on the construction component (not land). This is factored into developer pricing and is not an additional cost for buyers.

Section 07

Step-by-Step Buying Checklist

This checklist applies across all four markets. Print it out or save it as a PDF alongside this guide. Use it to track your progress from first inquiry to holding title.

Phase 1 — Research & Market Selection

  • Define your investment objective Rental income, capital appreciation, personal use, or retirement? Each market serves different goals. Review the comparison table above.
  • Set a realistic budget including all costs Add 3–5% to purchase price for closing costs, attorney fees, and initial setup (furnishings for rentals, utility connections, etc.).
  • Research target areas online before visiting Review local Facebook expat groups, international property listings (Point2Homes, Properati), and recent sales data.
  • Plan a scouting trip of at least 2 weeks Rent in your target area before buying. Seasonal variation in tourism areas means different months feel dramatically different.

Phase 2 — Legal and Financial Preparation

  • Hire a qualified local attorney before making any offer Never use an attorney recommended exclusively by your real estate agent. Seek independent referrals from expat communities or your home country embassy.
  • Consult a tax advisor in your home country Understand foreign property reporting requirements (FBAR/FATCA for US citizens, equivalent reporting for other nationalities) before purchasing.
  • Obtain local tax identification number CDI (Argentina), RUT (Chile), DIMEX/residency ID (Costa Rica), RUC (Nicaragua). Required for property registration in all four countries.
  • Establish how funds will be transferred internationally Wire transfers are standard. Confirm compliance requirements with both your sending bank and the receiving country's banking regulations. Argentine transactions may require specific channels — consult your attorney.
  • Determine ownership structure (individual vs. corporate entity) A local corporation can offer liability protection and estate planning benefits in Costa Rica, Nicaragua, and Argentina. Chile typically permits straightforward individual ownership without disadvantage.

Phase 3 — Due Diligence

  • Order a full title search through your attorney Verify chain of title, confirm no liens or encumbrances, check for boundary disputes. In Nicaragua, extend search to 30+ years. In Costa Rica, verify maritime zone status for coastal properties.
  • Confirm legal boundaries with a registered surveyor Cadastral maps and actual physical boundaries sometimes diverge. A certified survey is particularly important for land purchases and rural properties.
  • Conduct a physical inspection by a licensed engineer or inspector Tropical climates accelerate structural issues. Check for water damage, mold, foundation issues, and electrical/plumbing condition. Budget $300–$600 for professional inspection in most markets.
  • Verify utilities (water, power, internet) connectivity and cost Especially important in rural areas of Nicaragua, the Southern Zone of Costa Rica, and remote Lake District properties in Chile.
  • Research HOA/condo fees if purchasing in a managed development Monthly maintenance fees in gated communities and resort developments vary widely ($100–$800/month). Request 12 months of financial statements from the HOA board.

Phase 4 — Purchase and Closing

  • Negotiate and sign purchase agreement with your attorney present Ensure contingencies are included: clear title, satisfactory inspection, and financing (if applicable). Deposit (10% typical) should be held in attorney trust, not paid directly to seller.
  • Arrange international wire transfer of closing funds Allow 3–7 business days for international transfers to clear. Notify your bank in advance to avoid compliance delays on large transfers.
  • Attend closing or provide notarized power of attorney If you cannot be present at closing, your attorney can act under a properly executed power of attorney (POA). Remote closings are increasingly common and accepted in all four markets.
  • Confirm property registration and obtain title documents Verify your name (or entity name) appears correctly in the national property registry. Request certified copies of the recorded deed for your records.

Phase 5 — Post-Purchase

  • Obtain property insurance Structure, contents (for rentals), and liability coverage. International insurers (Lloyd's syndicates) operate in all four markets. Local coverage may be cheaper but review policy terms carefully.
  • Set up local bank account for operating expenses Simplifies payment of property taxes, utility bills, HOA fees, and property manager invoices. Required for tax compliance in some jurisdictions.
  • Engage a reputable property management company (for rentals) Interview at least 3 candidates. Review their current portfolio occupancy, maintenance response protocols, and owner reporting frequency. Full-service management typically costs 18–25% of gross rental income.
  • Register for local property tax and comply with annual filings Your local attorney can handle initial registration. Annual tax payments and rental income declarations (where required) should be calendared.
  • Update your home country tax filings to include foreign property US citizens must report foreign bank accounts (FBAR) and may need to file Form 8938. All nationalities should review foreign property reporting requirements with a qualified tax professional.
