Legal Tip: Foreigners Can Own Mexican Beachfront Property — Legally, for ~$600/Year

By International RE May 5, 2026 2 min read

One of the most common myths in Latin American real estate: "Foreigners can't own property on Mexico's coast." It's flat-out wrong. A legal structure called the fideicomiso trust lets any foreigner hold 100% beneficial ownership of beachfront property — for an annual fee of roughly $500–$800 USD.

What Is the Restricted Zone?

Mexican law prohibits direct foreign ownership of land within the "Restricted Zone" — defined as within 50 km of any coastline or 100 km of an international border. That covers Cancún, Los Cabos, Puerto Vallarta, Playa del Carmen, and most of the Mexican beach destinations that foreign buyers actually want.

The fideicomiso (bank trust) solves this entirely. A Mexican bank holds legal title as trustee — but you, the foreign buyer, are the sole beneficial owner. The bank cannot touch, sell, or encumber the property without your explicit instruction.

What You Actually Get

  • Full usage rights: Live in it, rent it short-term or long-term, renovate, improve
  • Full exit rights: Sell at any time by instructing the bank to transfer beneficial rights to the new buyer
  • Inheritance: Designate beneficiaries who assume your rights automatically — no Mexican probate
  • 50-year renewable term: Renew indefinitely — there's no practical limit on how long you hold the trust
  • No citizenship required: Any nationality can hold a fideicomiso

The Real Cost

Setup is a one-time cost of roughly $2,000–$3,000 (notary, bank establishment fee). After that, the annual trust maintenance fee runs $500–$800/year depending on the bank. For a property worth $300,000 or more, that's a rounding error — less than 0.3% of asset value per year to hold legal title.

Outside the Restricted Zone — in cities like Mexico City, Guadalajara, or San Miguel de Allende — foreigners can buy in their own name directly, with no trust required.

The Bottom Line

The fideicomiso structure has been in Mexican law since 1973 and is used by hundreds of thousands of foreign property owners today. It is not a workaround — it is the law. If anyone tells you foreigners can't own in Mexico, they don't know the rules.
