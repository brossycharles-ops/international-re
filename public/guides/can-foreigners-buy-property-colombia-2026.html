Can Foreigners Buy Property in Colombia? A Complete Legal Guide

By Sofia Mendez May 5, 2026 9 min read

The short answer: yes — and more cleanly than almost anywhere else in Latin America. Colombia's Political Constitution grants foreigners the same civil rights as Colombian nationals when it comes to property ownership. There are no restricted coastal zones, no mandatory bank trusts, and no special permits tied to your nationality. A foreign buyer can hold full, direct title to a property in Medellín, Cartagena, or Bogotá in exactly the same way a Colombian citizen would. The two steps that catch most foreign buyers off guard are not legal barriers at all — they are tax-registration requirements that are easy to handle once you know they exist.

The Constitutional Guarantee

Article 100 of Colombia's Constitution establishes that foreigners enjoy the same civil rights as Colombian nationals. Unlike Mexico, there is no "Restricted Zone" near coastlines or borders. Unlike Brazil, there are no caps on how much agricultural land a foreigner may hold in a single zone. In Colombia, the restrictions that do exist apply to specific land types — indigenous territories, national parks, and certain border-security zones — not to your passport. For any standard residential or commercial property in a city or tourist area, a foreigner buys under the same legal framework as a local.

Step 1 — Get a RUT (Tax ID)

Before you can sign a public deed in Colombia, you need a RUT (Registro Único Tributario), issued by Colombia's tax authority, the DIAN. This is your Colombian tax identification number and it is the cornerstone of any legal transaction in the country — property purchase, opening a bank account, or applying for residency. Non-residents can register for a RUT online through the DIAN portal or in person at a DIAN office; the process typically takes one to three business days and costs nothing. Your Colombian attorney can guide you through it, but it is administratively simple.

Step 2 — Register Your Foreign Funds (Formulario 4)

This is the step most foreign buyers skip and later regret. When you wire money from abroad to purchase Colombian property, you must register that capital transfer with Colombia's central bank, the Banco de la República, using Formulario 4 (the Foreign Investment Declaration form). Registration is straightforward — typically handled by your bank or attorney — but skipping it has serious consequences: you will be unable to legally repatriate your sale proceeds or rental income when you eventually exit the investment, and you will lose eligibility for Colombia's investor visa.

Register every wire transfer at the time it is made. Do not try to backfill Formulario 4 registrations after the fact; Colombian financial authorities take a dim view of retroactive compliance.

The Buying Process, Step by Step

Due Diligence and the Purchase Promise

Engage a Colombian property attorney (separate from any real estate agent) to search the Oficina de Registro de Instrumentos Públicos — Colombia's public property registry — for the property's certificado de libertad y tradición. This certificate shows the full chain of title, any outstanding liens or mortgages, and any encumbrances. Your attorney will also verify that the seller has no unpaid predial (property tax) or utility debt, which can travel with the property. Budget $1,000–$2,500 USD for a competent bilingual attorney in Medellín or Bogotá.

Once due diligence is satisfactory, the parties sign a promesa de compraventa — a binding purchase agreement — and the buyer pays a deposit, typically 10% of the purchase price. This document locks in the price, terms, and a closing timeline.

Signing the Escritura Pública

The final deed — the escritura pública — is executed before a Colombian notary public (Notaría). Both buyer and seller (or their attorneys-in-fact, with notarized powers of attorney) must appear. The notary authenticates the transfer and calculates the notary fees, registration tax, and applicable stamp duty owed at closing. Total time from signed promesa to completed registration: 30–60 days for a cash purchase.

Registration

Legal ownership transfers only once the deed is registered at the Oficina de Registro. Your notary submits the deed for registration; the turnaround is typically 5–15 business days. Until registration is confirmed, the transaction is not complete. Once registered, the new certificado de libertad y tradición will show your name as owner.

Closing Costs: What to Budget

Colombia's closing costs are among the most transparent in the region, and the buyer's share is relatively modest. Plan for total buyer-side closing costs of 2–3.5% of the purchase price. The main items:

  • Notary fees: Approximately 0.27% of the property value, split equally between buyer and seller (so each pays ~0.135%).
  • Registration tax (impuesto de registro): 1% of the deed value, paid entirely by the buyer in most departments.
  • Stamp tax (timbre): 1.5% on contracts above approximately COP 996 million (~$250,000 USD). For most residential transactions below this threshold, stamp tax does not apply.
  • Beneficencia (departmental surcharge): Roughly 0.5% of the property value.
  • Attorney fees: $1,000–$2,500 USD depending on complexity.
  • Appraisal (avalúo): Required for mortgage transactions; $200–$500 USD.

Seller-side costs include a capital gains tax (ganancia ocasional) of 15% on the profit from the sale, calculated as the difference between the cadastral value at acquisition and the sale price. As a buyer, this is not your liability — but understanding it helps you anticipate seller behaviour in negotiations.

Annual Property Tax: Low and Locally Set

Colombia's annual predial unificado (property tax) is levied by each municipality on the official cadastral value, which typically runs well below market value. Statutory rates range from 0.1% to 1.6% of cadastral value per year depending on property type and municipality. In practice, the effective tax on a mid-range Medellín apartment at market value often falls between 0.2% and 0.5% per year. A $200,000 USD apartment might generate an annual predial bill of $400–$1,000 USD. Taxes are typically due in the first half of the calendar year; many municipalities offer discounts of 10–15% for early payment.

Can Buying Property Lead to Residency?

Yes — and Colombia's investor visa threshold is one of the lowest in the region. The Visa M (Migrante) — Investor category requires a minimum real estate investment of 350 times the monthly legal minimum wage, which in 2026 translates to approximately COP 613 million (~$150,000 USD). This visa grants a two-year renewable stay and is a direct pathway to permanent residency (Visa R) after five consecutive years. Crucially, the investment must be registered as Foreign Direct Investment via Formulario 4 — another reason that step is non-negotiable. Mortgage Financing for Foreigners

Foreign nationals can obtain mortgage financing from Colombian banks, though terms are less favourable than for residents. Expect a loan-to-value (LTV) ratio of 50–70%, meaning a down payment of at least 30–50% of the purchase price, plus closing costs. Interest rates on peso-denominated mortgages have moderated from their 2023 highs but remain in the 10–14% range as of early 2026 — meaningful for cash-flow analysis. Most foreign investors purchasing for rental income or long-term appreciation do so with cash or offshore financing.

Restrictions to Know About

The restrictions that apply to foreigners in Colombia are narrow and specific:

  • Indigenous resguardos: Land held in indigenous collective territory is inalienable and cannot be purchased by anyone, foreign or Colombian.
  • National parks and protected areas: Private ownership within designated protected zones is prohibited.
  • Baldíos (state-owned rural land): Large-scale purchases of undeveloped rural land near borders involve additional scrutiny from Colombian land authorities and can be politically sensitive — seek specialist advice before pursuing this category.

For urban apartments, townhouses, and developed coastal properties — the assets most foreign buyers are actually looking at — none of these restrictions apply.

The Bottom Line

Colombia is one of the most foreigner-friendly property markets in Latin America, precisely because there is no structural complexity layered onto foreign ownership. You own the property outright, in your name, from day one. The rules that matter — the RUT, the Formulario 4 — are administrative, not structural. Get them right from the start, work with a competent bilingual attorney, and the transaction is straightforward.
