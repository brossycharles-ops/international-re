Can Foreigners Buy Property in Mexico? A Complete Legal Guide

By Marco Rios April 28, 2026 10 min read

The short answer: yes. Foreigners can and do own property throughout Mexico — from Tulum to Cabo to Mexico City — and have done so legally for decades. The catch most buyers don't fully understand until they're mid-deal: properties within 50 km of any coastline or 100 km of any international border fall inside Mexico's Restricted Zone, where a special legal structure called a fideicomiso (bank trust) is required for residential ownership. Get this right from the start and buying in Mexico is straightforward. Ignore it and you can find yourself in a legally precarious position.

The Restricted Zone: What It Is and Why It Exists

Article 27 of the Mexican Constitution prohibits foreigners from holding direct title to residential property within the Restricted Zone — defined as all land within 50 kilometers (31 miles) of any coastline and 100 kilometers (62 miles) of any land border. This constitutional restriction dates to post-revolutionary nationalism and cannot be overridden by contract or treaty.

In practical terms, nearly every destination that draws foreign buyers — the Riviera Maya, Los Cabos, Puerto Vallarta, Playa del Carmen, Mazatlán, Cancún — falls inside the Restricted Zone. So does most of the US-Mexico border region. Mexico City, Guadalajara, San Miguel de Allende, and Oaxaca City, however, are all outside the zone and can be purchased with direct title in a foreigner's name.

The Fideicomiso: How It Works

The fideicomiso is a bank trust established under Mexican banking law. A Mexican bank (the fiduciario) holds legal title to the property on the foreign buyer's behalf. The buyer is the trust beneficiary and retains all practical rights of ownership: the right to occupy, rent, renovate, sell, mortgage, and bequeath the property.

Key mechanics of the fideicomiso:

  • Duration: The initial trust term is 50 years, renewable indefinitely before expiration. You will never be forced to sell because a trust expires — renewal is routine.
  • Setup permit: The bank files for a permit from the Secretaría de Relaciones Exteriores (SRE) on your behalf. This is standard and almost always approved; budget 4–6 weeks for the SRE process.
  • Setup cost: Approximately $1,000–$2,000 USD as a one-time fee, typically included in the notary's closing statement.
  • Annual trust fee: The bank charges an ongoing maintenance fee of roughly $350–$650 USD per year. Fee varies by bank and property value.
  • Beneficiary transfer: You can change beneficiaries (i.e., sell the beneficial interest) without restructuring the trust. Heirs can be named as successor beneficiaries, simplifying inheritance across borders.

The fideicomiso is not a second-class ownership structure — it is the normal, legally robust mechanism through which hundreds of thousands of foreigners own beachfront property throughout Mexico.

Direct Title (Outside the Restricted Zone)

Outside the Restricted Zone, foreigners can take direct title to property in their personal name, just as a Mexican citizen would. There are no additional permits, trusts, or intermediaries required. This applies to inland cities and towns — including some of Mexico's most desirable expat markets like San Miguel de Allende and Oaxaca — and carries slightly lower closing costs since no fideicomiso setup fee applies.

Mexican Corporation: The Commercial Alternative

A 100% foreign-owned Mexican corporation can hold title to property in the Restricted Zone for commercial purposes — operating a rental business, hotel, boutique, or any income-generating use. However, a corporation cannot be used to circumvent the fideicomiso requirement for primary residential use by the foreign shareholders. The line between commercial and residential can be blurry; get legal advice specific to your intended use before choosing this route.

The Buying Process, Step by Step

Step 1: Hire an Independent Mexican Notary-Attorney

In Mexico, real estate closings are executed by a Notario Público — a government-appointed attorney with exclusive authority to authenticate property transfers. The notary is legally neutral but is engaged and paid primarily at closing. Hire your own independent attorney (separate from the notary) to conduct due diligence and represent your interests throughout. Budget $1,500–$3,000 for attorney fees on a standard transaction.

Step 2: Title Search and Due Diligence

Your attorney will search the Registro Público de la Propiedad (Public Property Registry) to verify: the seller holds clean title, no liens or encumbrances exist, property tax (predial) is current, and the cadastral description matches the physical property. For coastal properties, also confirm the lot does not encroach on federally owned beach land (zona federal marítimo terrestre), which is inalienable.

Step 3: Sign a Promissory Agreement and Fund Escrow

Once due diligence is satisfactory, buyer and seller execute a promesa de compraventa (promissory purchase agreement) and the buyer deposits earnest money — typically 5–10% of the purchase price — into a licensed escrow account. Use a reputable US-licensed escrow company operating in Mexico; sending funds directly to a seller or broker without escrow is a significant and avoidable risk.

Step 4: Bank Applies for SRE Permit (Restricted Zone Only)

For Restricted Zone purchases, your chosen bank files for the SRE permit and prepares the fideicomiso. This step typically takes 4–8 weeks and runs in parallel with your financing arrangements.

Step 5: Closing Before the Notary

Both parties (or attorneys-in-fact with notarized powers of attorney) appear before the notary. The deed is executed, funds released from escrow, and the transfer submitted to the Public Registry. Registry processing takes an additional 30–60 days, though legal ownership transfers at the signing date. Total timeline from signed promissory agreement to closing: 60–120 days for cash buyers; 3–5 months if a new fideicomiso must be established from scratch.

Closing Costs: What to Budget

Mexico's closing costs run higher than many buyers expect. Total costs for a foreign buyer typically fall between 7–10% of the purchase price inside the Restricted Zone, or 5–8% outside it. The main line items:

  • Acquisition Tax (ISAI): A state-level transfer tax of 2–5% of the declared value, depending on location. Quintana Roo (Riviera Maya, Cancún) charges 2%; Baja California Sur (Los Cabos) charges 3%; rates vary by municipality.
  • Notary fees: Approximately 1–1.5% of the property value, regulated by state fee schedules.
  • Public Registry and deed recording fees: Roughly 0.5–1% combined.
  • Fideicomiso setup fee: $1,000–$2,000 USD (Restricted Zone only).
  • Attorney fees: $1,500–$3,000 USD.
  • Appraisal (avalúo): $300–$700 USD, required by the notary.

Note: ISAI is assessed on the declared value, which must be at least the cadastral value. Historically, some transactions were under-declared to reduce taxes; Mexican tax authorities have tightened enforcement and this practice carries real legal risk.

Annual Property Tax: Low but Variable

Mexico's annual predial (property tax) is set by each municipality and calculated on the cadastral value — which often lags market values significantly. Effective rates commonly fall between 0.1% and 0.2% of market value per year, making Mexican property tax among the lowest in the Americas. A $300,000 beachfront condo might generate an annual tax bill of $300–$600 USD. Taxes are paid annually, in January.

Can Buying Property Lead to Residency?

Not automatically, but there is a clear path. Mexico's Investor Temporary Residency visa is available to foreigners who can demonstrate investment in Mexico — including real estate — with a minimum value of approximately $250,000 USD (the threshold is updated annually and tied to the UMA index). This grants temporary residency renewable annually, with a path to permanent residency after four years. Separately, foreigners with provable monthly income above ~$2,700 USD qualify for income-based temporary residency without a minimum property investment.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Using the seller's notary without independent counsel: A notary is not your advocate. Hire a separate attorney for due diligence and contract review.
  • Skipping the title search: Fraudulent and duplicate sales do occur, particularly with pre-construction projects. Verify title at the Registry independently.
  • Sending funds without escrow: Wire money only to a licensed escrow company, never directly to a developer, seller, or broker.
  • Misunderstanding the federal beach zone: The first 20 meters above the high-tide line on federal maritime zones is inalienable public land. No private ownership is possible, regardless of what any deed might say.
  • Ignoring the SRE permit timeline: In Restricted Zone purchases, the SRE permit process adds weeks. Budget for it in your timeline and don't agree to a closing date that doesn't allow for it.

The Bottom Line

Mexico's foreign ownership framework is mature, well-tested, and trusted by hundreds of thousands of foreign property owners. The fideicomiso is not a workaround or a compromise — it is a legally robust ownership structure that has functioned reliably for 40+ years. The real risks in Mexican real estate are not structural but transactional: undisclosed liens, fraud in pre-construction sales, and undeclared property values. Mitigate those with an independent attorney, a thorough title search, and a reputable escrow provider, and buying property in Mexico as a foreigner is a legitimate and well-worn path.
