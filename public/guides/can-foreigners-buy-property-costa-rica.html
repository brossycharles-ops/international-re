Can Foreigners Buy Property in Costa Rica? A Complete Legal Guide

By Sofia Mendez April 14, 2026 10 min read

The short answer: yes, absolutely. Costa Rica is one of the most foreigner-friendly real estate markets in the Western Hemisphere. Under Article 19 of the Costa Rican Constitution, non-citizens enjoy the same property rights as nationals — no local partner required, no special permits, no residency prerequisite. But there are rules worth knowing before you wire a deposit.

The Legal Foundation: Equal Rights for Foreign Buyers

Costa Rica's constitution explicitly guarantees that foreigners and citizens have identical property rights. This is not common in Latin America — Mexico, for instance, requires foreigners to purchase coastal or border-zone properties through a fideicomiso (bank trust). Costa Rica has no such restriction for standard titled properties.

You can buy in your personal name, in the name of a Costa Rican corporation (more on that below), or as joint owners with a spouse or partner. The property is registered in the Registro Nacional (National Registry), a public database that records all titled real estate transactions and encumbrances. Any attorney or member of the public can verify ownership and liens online.

Understanding Property Types: The Critical Distinction

Not all Costa Rican property is created equal. Before diving into the buying process, you need to understand the three main property categories — because they have very different legal implications.

1. Titled Property (Propiedad con Título)

This is standard freehold real estate, similar to fee-simple ownership in the US or Canada. The property has a unique cadastral number registered in the National Registry. Foreigners can own titled property with zero restrictions. This is the safest category and what most buyers should target.

2. Maritime Zone Property (Zona Marítimo Terrestre)

This is where things get complicated. Costa Rica's Maritime Zone Law (Law No. 6043, enacted 1977) governs all land within 200 meters of the ocean's mean high-tide line. It is divided into two sub-zones:

  • Public Zone (0–50 meters from high tide): Inalienable public land. No one — not citizens, not foreigners — can own this strip. It exists for public beach access.
  • Restricted Zone (50–200 meters from high tide): Can be leased from the municipality for up to 20 years (renewable), but cannot be owned by foreigners who have not held legal residency for at least five consecutive years. Citizens can hold these concessions; foreigners under five years of residency cannot hold them directly.

The practical implication: that Instagram-perfect beachfront lot with 30 meters to the waves is almost certainly in the Maritime Zone. A foreigner buying such a property would need to structure ownership through a Costa Rican corporation where citizens own at least 50% — or wait until they qualify for residency. Many buyers get tripped up here, so ask explicitly whether any property you're considering falls within 200 meters of the high-tide line.

3. Concession Land

Some coastal properties exist as municipal concessions rather than titled land. These can be valuable — many well-known resorts and beach communities sit on concession land — but they require additional due diligence. Confirm concession validity, municipal approval status, and whether the concession is transferable before making any offer.

The Buying Process, Step by Step

Step 1: Hire a Licensed Attorney

In Costa Rica, real estate transactions must be executed by a licensed notario público (notary public), who is also always a licensed attorney. Your attorney will perform the title search, draft the purchase agreement, and execute the transfer deed (escritura). Budget 1–1.5% of the purchase price for attorney fees, with a minimum of around $1,500 for most transactions.

Do not use the seller's attorney. Hire your own, independently. Referrals from your country's embassy in San José or from the Costa Rican Bar Association (Colegio de Abogados) are reliable starting points.

Step 2: Conduct Due Diligence

Your attorney should run a title study at the National Registry covering at minimum the past 30 years. This verifies:

  • The seller is the true registered owner
  • No liens, mortgages, or encumbrances exist
  • Property taxes are current (municipal tax arrears follow the property, not the seller)
  • The cadastral map (plano catastral) matches the physical property boundaries
  • No legal proceedings or lawsuits affect the title

Beyond the registry search, conduct on-the-ground diligence: verify utilities availability, zoning permits (uso de suelo), and whether any construction on the property was properly permitted. Unpermitted structures are common and can create complications at resale.

Step 3: Sign a Purchase Option Agreement

Once due diligence is satisfactory, buyer and seller sign an opción de compra (purchase option) or promesa de venta (promise of sale). This contract locks in the agreed price and terms, and typically accompanies an earnest money deposit of 5–10% of the purchase price held in escrow.

Use a licensed escrow company or a US-based escrow service that operates in Costa Rica. Sending funds directly to a seller without escrow protection is a significant risk — and a common source of buyer losses.

Step 4: Execute the Transfer Deed

The final closing occurs before a notary public, who prepares and authenticates the escritura pública (public deed). Both buyer and seller (or their attorneys-in-fact with power of attorney) must be present or represented. The deed is then submitted to the National Registry for registration, a process that typically takes 30–60 days to appear in the public record, though ownership legally transfers at signing.

Closing Costs: What to Budget

Costa Rica's closing costs are transparent and government-regulated. Expect to pay 4–7% of the purchase price total, broken down as follows:

  • Transfer tax: 1.5% of the registered value
  • Stamp duties (timbre fiscal, timbre de educación, etc.): approximately 0.55% combined
  • National Registry registration fee: ~0.2–0.5% on a sliding scale
  • Notary/attorney fees: 1–1.5% (regulated by the Bar Association)
  • Miscellaneous (surveys, due diligence, escrow): $500–$2,000

Note that transfer tax is calculated on the registered value, not necessarily the transaction price. Some sellers historically underregistered to reduce tax exposure — this practice is increasingly scrutinized by tax authorities and creates problems for buyers at resale.

Annual Property Tax: Remarkably Low

Costa Rica's annual property tax is 0.25% of the registered property value — among the lowest rates in the hemisphere. A $400,000 home would generate annual tax of just $1,000. Taxes are paid to the local municipality and are assessed on the registered cadastral value, which often lags behind market values, making effective rates even lower.

Properties valued above approximately $270,000 (the threshold adjusts annually) are also subject to a Luxury Home Tax (Impuesto Solidario), ranging from 0.25% to 0.55% on a progressive scale applied only to the value above the threshold. This affects higher-end purchases but is still modest by international standards.

Corporate Ownership: SRL vs. Sociedad Anónima

Many buyers — especially investors — purchase through a Costa Rican corporation rather than in their personal name. The two standard structures are the Sociedad Anónima (SA) and the Sociedad de Responsabilidad Limitada (SRL/Ltda). Both offer:

  • Liability protection separating personal assets from the property
  • Easier transfer of ownership (sell the company rather than the property, avoiding transfer tax)
  • Potential estate planning benefits for heirs in multiple jurisdictions
  • The ability for foreigners to hold Maritime Zone concessions (if citizen shareholders meet the 50%+ requirement)

Corporate formation costs around $500–$1,500 in attorney fees and takes 5–10 business days. Annual corporate taxes (impuesto de personas jurídicas) run approximately $400–$700 per year depending on declared capital, regardless of whether the company generates income.

A word of caution: transferring a property by selling the shares of a corporation avoids transfer tax but also means the buyer inherits any undisclosed liabilities of the company. Have your attorney conduct a full corporate due diligence alongside the property title search.

Financing Options for Foreign Buyers

Access to local bank financing is limited for non-residents. Costa Rican state banks (Banco Nacional, BCR, Banco Popular) primarily lend to residents and citizens. Private banks like BAC Credomatic and Scotiabank Costa Rica offer some foreigner-accessible mortgage products, but typically require:

  • 30–40% down payment
  • Proof of income from abroad
  • Clean credit history
  • Interest rates of 7–10% in USD or 10–14% in colones (as of early 2026)

Most foreign buyers pay cash or arrange financing in their home country (home equity loans, portfolio loans against existing assets) and wire funds to escrow. Developer financing on new-build projects — structured as stage payments during construction — is also common and bypasses the banking hurdle entirely.

Can Buying Property Lead to Residency?

Not automatically — but there's a clear path. Costa Rica's Investor Residency program grants temporary residency to foreigners who invest at least $150,000 in real estate (the threshold was raised from $50,000 in a 2021 reform). This residency, renewable every two years, allows you to live in Costa Rica indefinitely, work legally, and apply for permanent residency after three years. Permanent residency in turn qualifies you for Maritime Zone concession ownership.

Separately, the Rentista program (proof of $2,500/month passive income) and Pensionado program (proof of $1,000/month pension income) also grant residency without a property investment minimum — though many retirees combine a property purchase with a pensionado application.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Skipping the title search: Fraudulent sales do occur. Always verify ownership through the National Registry independently of what the seller shows you.
  • Ignoring Maritime Zone status: Beachfront doesn't always mean titled. Confirm whether any portion of the lot falls within 200 meters of high tide.
  • Sending money without escrow: Wire funds only to a licensed escrow company, never directly to a seller or broker.
  • Accepting unpermitted construction: Verify that structures have municipal permits. Unpermitted builds can be ordered demolished by authorities.
  • Not checking tax arrears: Unpaid property taxes attach to the land. Confirm the municipality shows a zero balance before closing.

The Bottom Line

Costa Rica's legal framework for foreign property ownership is genuinely among the best in Latin America. Full freehold title, equal constitutional rights, a transparent public registry, and rock-bottom annual property taxes make it a compelling destination for both investors and lifestyle buyers. The Maritime Zone rules add complexity for oceanfront purchases, but are manageable with competent legal counsel.

The single most important step: hire an independent, licensed notary-attorney before you sign anything or wire any funds. A thorough title search and proper escrow arrangement eliminate the vast majority of risk in a Costa Rican real estate transaction. Get those two things right, and buying property here as a foreigner is a straightforward process.
