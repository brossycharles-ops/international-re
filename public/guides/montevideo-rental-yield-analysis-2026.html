Montevideo Rental Yield Analysis 2026: What Investors Are Actually Earning

By Elena Voss April 24, 2026 9 min read

Montevideo doesn't make the headlines the way Medellín or Buenos Aires do — and that's quietly working in investors' favour. Uruguay's capital offers gross rental yields of 5–7% in its central barrios, vacancy rates of just 3–5%, and a legal framework that treats foreign buyers identically to citizens. Add a wave of incoming tech workers following Uruguay's 2025 innovation push, and the rental demand picture looks better than it has in years.

Why Montevideo Now

Uruguay has long been called the "Switzerland of South America" — stable currency, rule of law, low corruption, functioning institutions. What's changed recently is the demand side. In May 2025, the government launched the Uruguay Innovates program, a wide-ranging initiative to attract technology companies and high-skilled professionals to set up in Montevideo. Early results include several Latin American tech hubs relocating regional operations to the city, bringing with them waves of expatriate executives and engineers who typically rent for 12–24 months before deciding whether to buy.

That tenant pool — well-paid, internationally mobile, and accustomed to paying market rents — is landing in the exact neighborhoods most investors are targeting: Pocitos, Punta Carretas, and Cordón. Vacancy rates in those areas sit at approximately 3%, meaning well-priced units are rarely empty for more than a few weeks between tenancies.

Rental Yields by Neighborhood

Uruguay's property data is relatively transparent by regional standards. Here is a realistic yield picture across Montevideo's main investment zones as of early 2026:

Pocitos & Punta Carretas — 4.5%–5.5% gross

These are Montevideo's premium residential neighborhoods — tree-lined streets, beach access along the Rambla, dense café culture, and proximity to the Montevideo Shopping and World Trade Center office towers. Prices here run $3,500–$4,200/sqm for well-finished apartments, with new-build Vivienda Promovida units at the higher end. One-bedroom apartments rent for approximately $700–$950/month; two-bedrooms for $1,100–$1,600/month.

Yields compress here because capital appreciation has been strong. These neighborhoods suit investors who want low vacancy, quality tenants, and long-term price growth — not the highest current cash yield.

Cordón & Centro — 6%–7% gross

Cordón is the sweet spot for yield-focused investors. Prices drop to $2,700–$3,200/sqm while rental demand from university students, young professionals, and short-stay expats keeps occupancy high. The neighborhood is walkable, has strong café and restaurant density, and is undergoing visible gentrification pressure as overflow from Pocitos moves west. Two-bedroom units here typically rent for $900–$1,200/month, producing gross yields at the top of the central-area range.

Centro proper is cheaper still — $2,400–$2,800/sqm in older stock — but requires more careful selection. Some Centro blocks are office-heavy or poorly maintained. Target Ley Vivienda Promovida buildings from the past decade for newer stock and better tenants.

Carrasco — 4%–5% gross

Montevideo's wealthiest suburb sits at ~$4,350/sqm, the highest in the city. Rental demand exists but is thinner and more seasonal. Carrasco suits buyers purchasing for personal use or long-term family rental rather than pure yield maximisation. Don't buy here for cash flow.

Emerging Areas (Manga, Las Acacias, Colón) — 10%–13% gross

On paper, Manga posts gross yields around 13% and Casabo around 12%. In practice, these areas involve significantly higher operational risk — tenant arrears, property management challenges, and lower liquidity at resale. Investors unfamiliar with Montevideo should treat these figures with caution. A local property manager with experience in working-class barrios is non-negotiable if you go this route.

The Vivienda Promovida Advantage

Ley de Vivienda Promovida (Law 18.795) is the single most investor-friendly mechanism in the Uruguayan residential market. Under the scheme, developers who build qualifying apartments in designated urban renewal zones receive significant tax breaks — and pass some of those savings to buyers in the form of lower purchase prices. For buyers, qualifying units come with:

  • Exemption from property transfer tax (ITP) on the first purchase
  • IRPF exemption on rental income for a set number of years (typically 10)
  • IVA (VAT) reduction on the purchase price

The practical effect is that net yields on Vivienda Promovida units are often only marginally below gross yields during the exemption period, whereas unprotected rental income from conventional properties is taxed at 10.5% IRPF plus a small IRNR withholding for non-residents. The program's incentives were extended through September 2028 for qualifying projects — meaning a decent pipeline of new units will come to market over the next two years.

Foreign buyers are fully eligible for Vivienda Promovida units with no restrictions.

Purchase Costs and Entry Friction

Uruguay's closing costs are moderate and fully transparent:

  • Transfer tax (ITP): 2% of the registered value (waived for Vivienda Promovida first sales)
  • Notary fees: approximately 3% combined buyer/seller share
  • Registration and stamps: ~0.5%
  • Total buyer costs: roughly 4–5% on a conventional purchase; 1–2% on a Vivienda Promovida unit

Annual property taxes (Contribución Inmobiliaria) are low — typically 0.25%–0.35% of the cadastral value, which lags market values significantly. Effective annual tax on a $200,000 Pocitos apartment is often under $400/year.

Foreigners can buy in personal name or through a Uruguayan corporation (SA or SRL). Corporate ownership offers liability protection and can simplify cross-border estate planning. Foreign buyers do not need residency to purchase.

The Residency Upside

Uruguay's tax residency program is one of the most generous in the region. New residents receive an 11-year exemption on foreign-source income (the year of residency plus ten full calendar years). For investors who establish residency, this means foreign dividends, capital gains, and pension income are untaxed during the holiday period. The 2025–2029 National Budget Law updated the regime effective 2026 — investors who secured residency before December 31, 2025 are grandfathered under the previous rules; new applicants fall under the revised framework, which remains highly competitive.

Residency can be obtained by demonstrating a regular income from any source (no minimum property investment threshold), making it accessible to a wide range of investors and retirees.

Realistic Net Yield Estimate

For a Vivienda Promovida two-bedroom in Cordón purchased at $180,000 ($3,000/sqm for 60 sqm), renting for $1,050/month:

  • Gross annual rent: $12,600
  • Gross yield: 7.0%
  • Less management fee (8%): -$1,008
  • Less maintenance/reserve (1%): -$1,800
  • Less taxes (waived under Vivienda Promovida exemption): $0
  • Net yield: approximately 5.4%

That is a conservative estimate and assumes no vacancy. At 95% occupancy (one vacant month every 20), net yield holds around 5.1%. For a USD-denominated asset in a country with strong rule of law and a 16–28% cumulative price appreciation forecast over five years, that pencils attractively versus comparable markets in the region.

Key Risks to Underwrite

No market is without risk. Montevideo's main ones:

  • Peso depreciation: Rents are typically denominated in pesos, not USD. The UYU has historically devalued against the dollar, compressing USD-equivalent yields over time. Negotiate USD leases where possible, particularly with expat tenants.
  • Vivienda Promovida supply: The incentive program is producing new supply, which could cap rent growth in targeted neighborhoods over the next two to three years.
  • Liquidity: Montevideo is not a deep market. Resale can take six to twelve months for correctly priced properties; overpriced listings sit longer. Plan your exit horizon accordingly.

The Bottom Line

Montevideo offers one of the most credible risk-adjusted rental yield propositions in Latin America. The combination of low vacancy, a new wave of tech-sector tenant demand, a meaningful tax exemption through the Vivienda Promovida framework, and one of the region's most investor-friendly legal environments gives it a profile that deserves serious attention. Cordón and Centro are the value plays for yield; Pocitos and Punta Carretas for capital preservation and tenant quality. Either way, entry costs are low and the legal process is straightforward for foreign buyers.
