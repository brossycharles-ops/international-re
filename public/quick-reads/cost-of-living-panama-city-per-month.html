Cost of Living in Panama City Per Month (2026)

By James Whitfield April 24, 2026 3 min read

Panama City is the most expensive capital in Central America — but expensive is relative. A single expat living well here in 2026 spends roughly $1,800–$2,200 per month all-in, according to current Numbeo and Expatistan data. A couple comfortable with local habits can land closer to $2,800–$3,200. Here is where that money actually goes.

The Biggest Line Item: Rent

Housing drives most of the variation in expat budgets. In established neighbourhoods like El Cangrejo, Marbella, and San Francisco, a furnished one-bedroom apartment runs $800–$1,200/month. Step up to Costa del Este or Punta Pacifica — Panama's premium high-rise zones — and the same footprint costs $1,400–$2,000+.

Renters who are willing to live in Casco Viejo or outer districts like Albrook can find furnished studios for $600–$800, with the trade-off of longer commutes. Unlike some Latin American capitals, landlords in Panama City routinely quote rents in USD — there is no currency risk on lease costs.

Food, Dining, and Groceries

Grocery bills for a single person run $200–$350/month if you cook at home and use local markets alongside supermarkets. The Riba Smith chain is the go-to for imported goods and is priced accordingly. El Rey and Super 99 are cheaper for local staples.

Eating out is where Panama City shows its range. A meal at a local fonda (working-class lunch counter) costs $4–$7. A sit-down lunch at a mid-range restaurant in Marbella runs $12–$20 per person. Dinner at a proper restaurant in Punta Pacifica will easily reach $35–$60 per person with drinks. Most expats eat a mix and land around $300–$500/month on food total.

Transport

Panama City's Metro costs $0.35 per ride — one of the cheapest urban rail systems in the Americas. The network now covers two lines connecting Albrook to Tocumen airport and into the suburbs, making car-free living genuinely viable in central districts. Budget under $30/month if you rely on Metro plus occasional Uber. Keeping a car adds $150–$250/month in fuel, parking, and insurance.

Healthcare

Private healthcare is Panama City's standout value. A general practitioner visit at a private clinic costs $30–$60. Specialist consultations range from $60–$120. Local private health insurance policies start around $100–$150/month for a healthy adult under 50 — significantly less than comparable US or European coverage. Hospital Nacional and Clinica Hospital San Fernando are the two private hospitals most expats use.

The Full Monthly Picture

  • Rent (1-bed, mid-tier area): $900–$1,200
  • Groceries + dining: $350–$500
  • Transport: $30–$60 (Metro) or $200–$250 (car)
  • Health insurance: $100–$150
  • Utilities (electric, water, internet): $100–$180
  • Entertainment, misc: $200–$300
  • Total (single person): $1,780–$2,390

What Panama City Offers That the Numbers Don't Capture

Panama uses the US dollar as its official currency, which eliminates exchange-rate risk entirely — a meaningful advantage over markets like Colombia or Argentina where currency volatility complicates long-term budgeting. The country also has no capital gains tax on property and one of the most straightforward residency pathways in the region via the Friendly Nations Visa.

For property investors, Panama City's rental market is tight enough in prime zones that a well-located apartment can cover a meaningful portion of carrying costs. See our Panama foreign buyer legal guide and the Panama City vs. San José comparison for the full investment picture.
