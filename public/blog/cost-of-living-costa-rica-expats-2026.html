Cost of Living in Costa Rica 2026: Honest Numbers for Expats

By International RE  ·  April 30, 2026  ·  11 min read

Costa Rica has been marketed as an affordable retirement paradise for thirty years. The reality in 2026 is more nuanced: it's affordable relative to the US, Canada, and Western Europe, but it's the most expensive country in Central America and noticeably pricier than Colombia, Ecuador, Mexico, or Panama for most categories of spending. The expats who are happiest in Costa Rica either knew this going in — or arrived with enough cushion that it didn't matter.

This guide is honest about costs. No cherry-picked budgets, no omitting HOA fees or vehicle costs. Four cities, real monthly numbers, and the hidden costs most guides skip.

What Drives Costs in Costa Rica

Before the numbers, understanding the structural cost drivers helps you budget accurately:

  • IVA (13% VAT) on most goods and services. Costa Rica's value-added tax applies to almost everything: restaurants, services, electronics, clothing. This adds up quickly and is baked into every price you see.
  • High import taxes on vehicles. A new car that costs $25,000 in the US retails for $40,000–$60,000 in Costa Rica after import duties. Used cars are similarly priced up. This is often the biggest surprise for new arrivals who plan to drive.
  • The colón is stable but not pegged. The CRC has been relatively stable against the USD for the past decade (roughly 500–530 CRC/USD), which means your costs in USD terms are predictable. But it's not Panama or Ecuador — there is some FX exposure.
  • Beach towns carry a premium. Tamarindo, Santa Teresa, Manuel Antonio, and Nosara all command 30–60% rent premiums over the Central Valley for equivalent quality. Tourism demand and limited land supply drive this.
  • The CAJA makes healthcare dramatically cheaper than the US. Costa Rica's public healthcare system is one of the best in Latin America. Residents can join CAJA for $75–$150/month and access full healthcare. This is a genuine cost advantage over the US — a major factor for retirement budgets.

The Car Cost No One Mentions

Every guide lists monthly expenses but skips the vehicle cost. In Costa Rica outside San José, a car is not optional — it's a necessity. Importing a car from the US or buying locally, you'll spend $20,000–$45,000 for a reliable 4WD vehicle (essential in Guanacaste and on the southern Pacific). This is a one-time cost, but amortized over 5 years, it adds $300–$750/month to your effective cost of living that most "monthly budget" guides omit entirely.

Healthcare Costs in Detail

This is where Costa Rica genuinely delivers vs. North America:

  • CAJA (public system): Legal residents can enroll for $75–$150/month. Full coverage including specialists, hospitalization, prescriptions, and surgery. Wait times exist for non-emergency specialist care (1–6 weeks typically). Emergency care is immediate.
  • Private healthcare: Full private health insurance: $150–$250/month for a 50-year-old. GP visit: $40–$80. Specialist: $80–$150. Major surgery: $5,000–$30,000 depending on procedure (vs. $30,000–$200,000+ in the US).
  • Dental: A crown in Costa Rica: $400–$600. Root canal: $250–$400. Full dental tourism is common — US residents fly to San José, get $5,000 of dental work done for $1,500, and save enough to cover the flight and hotel multiple times over.
  • The best strategy for most expats: Join CAJA for major coverage ($75–$150/month), add a supplemental private insurance policy for convenience and speed ($100–$150/month extra). Total healthcare budget: $200–$300/month per person, significantly under most US health insurance premiums alone.

Costa Rica costs more than Colombia, Ecuador, and Argentina — but comes with stronger rule of law, better English penetration, closer proximity to the US (5-hour flight from Miami), and a stable democracy that has had no army since 1948. Many expats consider the premium worthwhile for these intangibles.

The Pensionado Visa: Key Numbers

Costa Rica's Pensionado visa requires $1,000/month in pension income (government or private). For permanent residency, you need 3 years of legal residency. Citizenship requires 7 years total residency and Spanish language proficiency.

The Rentista visa requires $2,500/month in passive income and a $60,000 deposit at a Costa Rican bank for 5 years. More onerous than the Pensionado — most retirees target the pension route.

The Bottom Line

Costa Rica is not the cheapest option in Latin America — not by a significant margin. If minimizing monthly spend is the primary goal, Ecuador, Colombia, or Argentina offer more purchasing power per dollar. What Costa Rica offers instead: political stability, English accessibility, excellent private healthcare, proximity to the US, a large established expat community, and a quality of life that is hard to replicate elsewhere in the region.

For the right buyer — someone who values stability and ease over pure cost efficiency — the premium is worth it. For pure budget retirees, the math points elsewhere.
