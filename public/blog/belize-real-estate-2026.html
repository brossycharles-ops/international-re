Belize Real Estate 2026: Ambergris Caye, Caye Caulker & the Honest Investor Guide

By International RE  ·  April 30, 2026  ·  12 min read

Belize punches well above its weight as a real estate destination. A country of 450,000 people, it has three features no other Central American nation can offer simultaneously: English as the official language, the US dollar accepted universally (the Belizean dollar is pegged 2:1 to USD), and zero capital gains tax on real estate. For North American retirees and investors who want Caribbean beachfront without navigating a foreign language or currency risk, Belize is often the answer hiding in plain sight.

The caveats are real — infrastructure is thin outside the caye islands, property prices on Ambergris Caye have risen 40–60% since 2019, and the market is small enough that resale liquidity can be slow. This guide gives you the full picture: where to buy, what to pay, what yields to expect, and what the QRP retirement program actually offers in 2026.

Why Belize: The Structural Case

Most North American buyers who end up in Belize arrive via one of three routes: they googled "Caribbean real estate English speaking," they heard about the QRP visa program, or they came on a diving trip to the Barrier Reef and never fully left. All three paths lead to the same market dynamics.

The language advantage is real. In Panama, Costa Rica, Colombia, and Mexico, navigating real estate — attorneys, contracts, HOA disputes, contractor bids — requires either Spanish or a reliable bilingual intermediary. In Belize, all legal documents are in English, contracts are in English, the court system operates in English, and almost everyone in the tourism industry is fluent. The friction of being a foreign buyer is substantially lower.

The currency peg eliminates uncertainty. The Belizean dollar has been pegged at 2:1 to the USD since 1978 — nearly 50 years of unbroken peg. In practice, USD cash and USD bank transfers are accepted everywhere on the cayes. Your rental income, your sale proceeds, your HOA fees — all quoted and settled in USD. This is a different category from Panama (which uses the dollar outright) but functionally close: currency risk is near-zero.

No capital gains tax is a significant compounding advantage. If you buy a caye property today for $250,000 and sell it in 10 years for $400,000, you keep the full $150,000 gain. In Mexico (with fideicomiso and ISAI considerations), Panama, Colombia, or Costa Rica, a portion would be taxed. Belize's zero-CGT policy combined with low annual holding costs means your net IRR on a successful exit is higher than in comparable markets.

Where to Buy: The Key Zones

Ambergris Caye — The Primary Market

Belize's largest island and undisputed centre of the expat and tourism real estate market. San Pedro town is the commercial hub — restaurants, bars, dive shops, and the majority of the island's condo inventory. North Ambergris (north of the bridge from San Pedro) is where the upscale developments, beachfront villas, and resort-style condos are concentrated. Price range: $180,000–$800,000+ for condos; $400,000–$3M+ for beachfront villas. Gross STR yields: 8–12% for well-managed condos with strong Airbnb presence. The island has seen significant development since 2019 — new supply is coming onto the market and increasing competition in the rental pool. Research occupancy rates at specific buildings before buying.

Caye Caulker — The Value Alternative

Smaller, slower, and cheaper than Ambergris. Known for the "Go Slow" culture — no cars, golf carts only, backpacker energy. Entry prices are 30–50% below Ambergris: condos from $80,000–$200,000. The tourist base is younger and more budget-conscious, which means lower ADRs ($60–$120/night) but also lower capex and operating costs. For buyers who want beach exposure without Ambergris price tags, Caye Caulker is the alternative — with the trade-off of lower-income tourism and less resale liquidity.

Placencia Peninsula — Best for Low-Density Lifestyle

A 16-mile peninsula on the mainland southern coast. Increasingly popular with retirees who want more land, privacy, and a quieter lifestyle than the cayes offer. Prices range from $80,000 for small lots to $500,000+ for beachfront homes. The Placencia Village to Maya Beach corridor has the best infrastructure. Airbnb yields exist but are lower than Ambergris (the tourism volume isn't there yet). This is a lifestyle-first, yield-secondary market.

Corozal — The Budget Retirement Zone

Northern Belize, 15 minutes from the Mexican border. Not a beach market — Corozal Bay is calmer and less dramatic than the cayes. But it's where you find the cheapest real estate in Belize ($50,000–$150,000 for houses) and the highest concentration of budget-conscious North American retirees living on QRP visas. Cost of living is genuinely low: couples report $1,500–$2,000/month all-in. Trade-offs: limited medical facilities, limited entertainment, and you're essentially in Mexico's orbit rather than a Caribbean island.

The QRP Program: What It Actually Offers

Belize's Qualified Retired Persons (QRP) program is one of the most straightforward retirement visa programs in the Americas. Key details for 2026:

  • Age requirement: 45 years and older (no upper limit)
  • Income requirement: $2,000/month from outside Belize (pension, Social Security, investment income, rental income from foreign property)
  • Status granted: Immediate permanent residency — not temporary, not tiered. You are a permanent resident from day one.
  • Key benefits:
    • Duty-free import of personal and household effects (one-time)
    • Duty-free import of one vehicle, boat, or small aircraft (one-time)
    • No tax on income earned outside Belize
    • No capital gains tax
  • Restrictions: QRP holders cannot work for wages in Belize (though they can own and run a business). Income must genuinely originate outside Belize.
  • Processing time: 2–3 months. Government fee: approximately $1,000 USD. Immigration attorney: $500–$1,000.
  • Citizenship path: Permanent residency from QRP does not directly lead to citizenship — you need 5 years of standard permanent residency for that. However, most QRP holders are not seeking citizenship.

QRP vs. Other Regional Retirement Visas

Ecuador's Jubilado requires only $500/month but requires basic Spanish and more bureaucracy. Panama's Pensionado requires $1,000/month. Costa Rica's Pensionado requires $1,000/month. Belize's QRP requires $2,000/month — higher threshold — but delivers immediate permanent residency (not a 2-year renewable), English-language process, and the duty-free import benefit that can save $20,000–$50,000 on a vehicle and household goods alone. For buyers with $2,000+/month in income who want the simplest possible English-language process, QRP is compelling.

The Buying Process

Belize follows English common law (it gained independence from Britain in 1981 and retained the legal framework). The process is familiar to North American buyers:

  1. Offer and acceptance: Letter of intent with deposit (typically 10%). No binding obligation yet.
  2. Attorney engagement: Hire a Belizean attorney ($1,500–$3,000 flat fee). They conduct title search at the Lands Registry.
  3. Sale agreement: Binding contract. Balance typically due at closing.
  4. Title transfer: New land certificate issued in buyer's name. Stamp duty (5%) and registration fees paid at this stage.
  5. Timeline: 30–60 days from offer to title transfer.

Leasehold vs. freehold: Some properties in Belize — particularly on public or Cayes land — are held under 99-year leases from the government rather than freehold title. Leasehold is generally considered safe but is not equivalent to freehold ownership. Verify freehold status before buying. Your attorney's title search will reveal this clearly.

Honest Risks

  • Market size and liquidity: Belize is a small market. When you want to sell, the buyer pool is limited — primarily North American expats and retirees. Budget 6–18 months to find a buyer at full price. This is not Panama City with 12,000 transactions/year.
  • Infrastructure: Power outages on the cayes are real, though most quality developments have generator backup. Water pressure issues exist. Internet has improved significantly (fiber now available in San Pedro) but remains less reliable than the mainland US.
  • Rising prices may compress future yields: Ambergris prices rose 40–60% between 2019 and 2025. New development is adding supply. Future price appreciation is unlikely to repeat at that rate; yield compression is possible if supply continues outpacing demand growth.
  • Hurricane exposure: Belize sits in the Caribbean hurricane belt. Insurance is available but expensive. Structural construction quality and storm-rated construction matter — verify before buying. Most quality developments are built to hurricane standards, but older structures vary.
  • Healthcare: San Pedro has a small medical centre. Serious medical issues require evacuation to Belize City or Mexico. Not suitable for buyers who need regular specialist access or who have significant health concerns.

Belize wins on language, currency stability, and CGT. It loses on pure yield ceiling (the DR's Punta Cana market can hit 14%) and liquidity (thin resale market vs. the DR's high transaction volume). The right choice depends on your priorities: if English and tax efficiency matter most, Belize is hard to beat in the Caribbean. If yield maximization is the goal, Punta Cana or Tulum will outperform on gross numbers.
