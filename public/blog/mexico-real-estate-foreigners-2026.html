Mexico Real Estate for Foreigners 2026: The Complete Legal Guide

By International RE April 29, 2026 15 min read

Mexico is the most popular destination for American and Canadian expat buyers in the world — and it's not close. Cabo, Puerto Vallarta, Playa del Carmen, Tulum, San Miguel de Allende, Mexico City: these markets have absorbed tens of billions in foreign investment. Prices have appreciated 30–60% in peso terms since 2020, and 10–20% in USD terms in the most sought-after coastal zones.

Yet the single biggest mistake foreign buyers make is misunderstanding the ownership structure. The fideicomiso trust requirement in coastal and border areas isn't a restriction — it's a well-established legal mechanism that gives you full beneficial ownership. Get it right, and Mexico is one of the world's most accessible foreign property markets. Get it wrong, and you're exposed.

This guide covers everything: the legal structure, real closing costs, the best markets by strategy, and how to protect yourself in 2026.

Bottom line up front: Foreigners can buy property anywhere in Mexico. Coastal and border areas require a fideicomiso bank trust (~$700/year to maintain). Interior areas allow direct ownership. There are no foreign buyer taxes, no ownership caps, no required visa. Total closing costs: 6–10%.

The Restricted Zone: What It Is and What It Means

Mexico's constitution prohibits direct foreign ownership within the Restricted Zone — defined as within 100 km of any international border and within 50 km of any coastline. This covers almost every beach destination foreigners want to buy in: Cancún, Tulum, Cabo, Puerto Vallarta, Riviera Nayarit, Los Cabos, Huatulco, etc.

The solution is the fideicomiso (bank trust). Outside the Restricted Zone — Mexico City, San Miguel de Allende, Guadalajara, Querétaro, the Bajío region — foreigners own directly with a standard deed.

⚠ Restricted Zone (50km coast / 100km border)

Tulum, Playa del Carmen, Cancún, Los Cabos, Puerto Vallarta, Mazatlán, Huatulco, border cities. Ownership via fideicomiso trust or Mexican corporation. You have full beneficial rights — use, rent, sell, inherit. Cost: ~$700/year bank fee.

✓ Free Zone (interior Mexico)

Mexico City, San Miguel de Allende, Guadalajara, Querétaro, Oaxaca City, Mérida (city center). Direct ownership with escritura deed. No trust required, no annual fees. Same closing process as any Mexican buyer.

The Fideicomiso: How It Actually Works

The fideicomiso is a real estate trust authorized by Mexico's Foreign Investment Law. Here's the structure:

  • Trustee: A Mexican bank (BBVA, Banamex, Santander, Scotiabank) holds legal title to the property
  • Beneficiary: You (the foreign buyer) hold beneficial title with all rights: use, lease, improve, sell, or pass to heirs
  • Term: 50 years, renewable indefinitely for another 50 years
  • Setup cost: $500–1,000 (one-time, paid at closing)
  • Annual maintenance: $500–700/year to the bank
  • Selling: You assign the trust beneficiary rights to the new buyer — same process as a direct sale

The fideicomiso is not a lease. It is full beneficial ownership. You are not "renting" through the bank. You can renovate, rent on Airbnb, sell at any price, and leave the property to your children — all without bank permission beyond administrative paperwork.

The alternative to a fideicomiso in the Restricted Zone is forming a Mexican corporation (S.A. de C.V. or S.R.L.) to hold the property. This is typically used for commercial properties or when you own multiple units. Corporations have more annual accounting obligations and are generally not recommended for single residential properties.

The Step-by-Step Buying Process

  1. Find a property and negotiate Prices are quoted in USD for most tourist/expat markets. Negotiate directly or through a buyer's agent. Mexican real estate has no MLS — many good properties are off-market.
  2. Letter of Intent / Offer A simple letter of intent locks in price and terms while due diligence proceeds. Not legally binding but establishes good faith. Accompanied by a $5,000–10,000 good-faith deposit (refundable if title issues found).
  3. Hire a notario público Mexico's notario is a state-appointed attorney who verifies title, checks for liens (gravámenes), confirms property taxes are current, and drafts the deed. Both buyer and seller use the same notario — unlike the US system. The buyer typically pays the notario's fee.
  4. Fideicomiso permit (if in Restricted Zone) The notario or bank files a permit application with Mexico's Secretaría de Relaciones Exteriores (SRE). Takes 3–6 weeks. Once approved, the bank is authorized as trustee.
  5. Purchase contract (Promesa de Compraventa) The formal purchase agreement specifying price, payment schedule, closing date, and conditions. Typically requires 10–30% deposit at signing. Read this carefully — cancellation penalties apply if you walk away without a legitimate contingency.
  6. Title search and appraisal (avalúo) Notario runs a 10-year title search. Bank or government-licensed appraiser values the property (affects acquisition tax calculation). Takes 2–4 weeks.
  7. Closing (escrituración) All parties sign the final deed before the notario. Remaining balance is wired (typically to escrow account at a Mexican bank or title company). The notario registers the deed with the Public Property Registry.

Total timeline: 6–12 weeks from accepted offer to recorded deed, assuming no title issues.

Title insurance is not standard in Mexico but is strongly recommended. Mexican title searches can miss ejido (communal land) encumbrances, incomplete intestate successions, or unpaid municipal fees that surface only after purchase. Stewart Title and First American both operate in Mexico's major markets. Cost is ~$500–1,500 on a $200K property.

STR Yield: 5–9% gross

USD-denominated luxury market. High average daily rates ($300–600/night). Less appreciation runway than Riviera Maya but very stable high-end demand. Best for wealth preservation + yield.

Puerto Vallarta

$1,600–4,000/sqm
STR Yield: 5–8% gross

Largest established expat community on the Pacific coast. Marina Vallarta, Conchas Chinas, El Centro each have distinct profiles. Strong long-term rental demand, mature market with good liquidity.

Mexico City (CDMX)

$1,500–4,000/sqm
LTR Yield: 4–6% gross

No fideicomiso needed. Polanco, Condesa, Roma Norte all have strong digital nomad and expat demand. Long-term rental market is deep. Nearshoring boom driving corporate demand in Santa Fe and Insurgentes corridor.

San Miguel de Allende

$1,800–4,500/sqm
STR Yield: 6–10% gross

UNESCO colonial city with America's largest expat retirement community. No fideicomiso. Colonial homes and boutique hotels. Strong vacation rental demand from US art/culture travelers. Limited supply — low-rise zoning.

FAQ: Mexico Real Estate for Foreigners

Can foreigners own property in Mexico?

Yes. Foreigners can own property anywhere in Mexico. In coastal/border Restricted Zones, ownership is through a fideicomiso bank trust. In interior Mexico (CDMX, San Miguel, Guadalajara), foreigners own directly with a standard deed.

What is a fideicomiso trust in Mexico?

A fideicomiso is a real estate bank trust where a Mexican bank holds legal title on your behalf. You hold all beneficial rights — use, rent, sell, inherit. It costs ~$500–1,000 to set up and ~$500–700/year to maintain. It lasts 50 years and is renewable indefinitely.

What are the closing costs when buying in Mexico?

Budget 6–10% of purchase price: acquisition tax (ISAI) 2–4.5%, notario fees 0.5–1%, fideicomiso setup ~$1,000, appraisal ~$400, title insurance ~$600. Agent commissions (5–8%) are typically seller-paid in Mexico.

Do I need a visa to buy property in Mexico?

No. You can purchase as a tourist. Residency is not required to own real estate. If you want to live in Mexico long-term, a Temporal Residente visa (renewable, income/savings qualified) is the standard path.

Is Tulum real estate a good investment in 2026?

Tulum offers 8–14% gross STR yields and strong appreciation (40%+ since 2020). Risks: large supply pipeline, infrastructure gaps, and active STR management requirements. Best for hands-on investors who can vet developers and manage occupancy actively. Not suitable for passive, hands-off buyers.
