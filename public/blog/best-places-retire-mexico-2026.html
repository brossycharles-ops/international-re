Best Places to Retire in Mexico 2026: 8 Cities Ranked for Expats

April 29, 2026 · 13 min read · By International RE Research

Over 1.5 million Americans and Canadians currently live in Mexico — the largest expat community in the Western Hemisphere. The combination of proximity to the US, low cost of living, warm climate, and a mature expat infrastructure makes Mexico the default first choice for North American retirees exploring a move abroad.

But "retire in Mexico" covers a lot of ground. San Miguel de Allende and Cancún are both in Mexico, and they're about as similar as Paris and Ibiza. This guide ranks the top 8 retirement destinations on the metrics that actually matter.

How we ranked these cities: Cost of living (couple), healthcare quality and access, safety record, English-language availability, expat community size, climate, and proximity to US airports. Each city is assessed for who it's actually best for — there's no single winner.

San Miguel is Mexico's most celebrated expat city for a reason: it's genuinely beautiful, entirely walkable, has a massive English-speaking community, world-class restaurants, good private hospitals, and a year-round spring climate at altitude. It's not the cheapest option, but it delivers a lifestyle that's hard to replicate anywhere in the world at this price.

The expat community (estimated 10,000–15,000 North Americans) means you'll never lack for English-speaking social infrastructure — book clubs, art classes, volunteer organizations, golf, language exchanges. The flip side is that San Miguel has become expensive by Mexican standards precisely because so many foreigners have moved there.

Lake Chapala has the largest concentration of North American retirees in the world outside the US — estimated 20,000–30,000 expats in the Ajijic–Chapala–Jocotepec corridor. National Geographic has called it one of the world's best climates (mild temperatures year-round, low humidity, 300+ sunny days). It's also noticeably cheaper than San Miguel.

The downside: the lakeside communities are aging infrastructure-wise, the lake itself has significant pollution issues, and the area is extremely car-dependent. Healthcare requires driving to Guadalajara (45 min) for anything serious.

Puerto Vallarta is the original Mexican beach retirement town — established enough to have excellent healthcare (CMQ Hospital is JCI-accredited), strong English infrastructure, and direct US flights on multiple airlines. The Banderas Bay setting is genuinely stunning. The LGBTQ+ community is particularly large and well-integrated.

Costs have risen with popularity — Zona Romántica and Versalles are no longer cheap. The rainy season (June–October) brings daily downpours and high humidity. But for beach-first retirees, PV remains the gold standard.

Mérida is consistently ranked Mexico's safest large city and is growing faster than any other expat destination on this list. Its colonial centro is stunning, the food scene (Yucatecan cuisine is arguably Mexico's best regional cooking) is world class, and it's the closest major Mexican city to the US East Coast. The Yucatán Peninsula's cenotes, beaches (1 hour away), and Mayan ruins are incredible day-trip territory.

The main challenge: Mérida is genuinely hot. June–September temperatures regularly exceed 95°F with high humidity. AC is non-negotiable and adds to utility bills. But it's the best value-for-quality city on this list for retirees who can handle the heat.

Oaxaca offers the lowest costs of any quality retirement destination in Mexico, a UNESCO-heritage colonial city, the world's best food scene (per multiple rankings), and a wonderfully mild climate. The expat community is smaller and more boutique — you'll encounter more artists, writers, and food-obsessed retirees than golf-playing snowbirds.

Healthcare is the main concern — Oaxaca has private clinics but lacks the hospital infrastructure of San Miguel or PV. Serious medical events may require travel to Mexico City (1-hour flight).

Mexico Retirement Visa: What You Need to Qualify

Mexico offers two residency tracks for retirees. Neither requires a job offer — they're purely income and asset based.

Visa TypeIncome RequirementDurationPath to Permanent
Residente Temporal~$2,600/mo USD (or ~$43K savings)1 year, renewable to 4Apply for permanent after 4 years
Residente Permanente~$3,500/mo USD (or ~$175K savings)IndefiniteAlready permanent — no renewal

Social Security qualifies: A US couple receiving $3,000–4,000/month combined Social Security income typically meets the Residente Temporal threshold. The application is made at the Mexican consulate in your home country, then finalized at an immigration office (INM) in Mexico within 30 days of arrival.

Healthcare for Retirees in Mexico

Private healthcare in Mexico is excellent in major cities and remarkably affordable. IMSS (public health insurance) is available to legal residents via voluntary enrollment for roughly $400–600/year — a bargain, though quality and wait times vary by facility. Most retirees combine IMSS enrollment with a private insurance policy for specialist access and private hospitals.

What is the best place to retire in Mexico in 2026?

For most US and Canadian retirees, the top overall choice is San Miguel de Allende (walkable, large English-speaking community, excellent climate, good hospitals). For best value, Lake Chapala/Ajijic wins — lowest costs, largest expat community, world-class climate. For beach retirement, Puerto Vallarta. For safety and proximity to the US East Coast, Mérida.

How much money do you need to retire in Mexico?

A comfortable retirement in Mexico costs $1,500–2,500/month for a couple in 2026. Budget-focused couples in Lake Chapala or Mérida can live well on $1,500–2,000/month including rent, food, healthcare, and entertainment. San Miguel and Puerto Vallarta run $2,000–3,500/month for a comfortable lifestyle. Social Security income alone is often sufficient.

What visa do I need to retire in Mexico?

The Residente Temporal visa requires approximately $2,600/month income (or ~$43,000 in savings) for 2026. After 4 years, you can apply for Permanent Residency. Many retirees enter on a tourist visa initially and then apply for residency from within Mexico. The application process takes 2–4 weeks and can be completed at any Mexican INM office.

Is Mexico safe for retirees in 2026?

Safety varies dramatically by location. Mérida, San Miguel de Allende, Lake Chapala/Ajijic, and the tourist zones of Puerto Vallarta all have excellent safety records for expats. Check the US State Department's Mexico travel advisory map — most top expat destinations are in Level 1 (Exercise Normal Precautions) or Level 2 zones. Avoid states with active cartel advisories.
