Cost of Living in Medellín 2026: The Real Numbers

By International RE April 29, 2026 12 min read

Medellín is the most talked-about expat city in Latin America — and the cost of living data online is mostly outdated, wrong, or produced by people who live in expensive El Poblado and think that's representative. This guide uses real 2026 numbers across all four main expat neighborhoods.

The short version: a single person can live well in Medellín for $1,200–1,800/month. A couple sharing expenses can hit $1,500–2,200/month comfortably. If you're earning $3,000+/month remotely, you will feel wealthy. Here's exactly where the money goes.

Exchange rate anchor (2026): 1 USD ≈ 4,000–4,200 COP (Colombian peso). Prices below are in USD at this rate. The peso has been relatively stable since 2024 — budget a 5–10% cushion for fluctuation.

Rent: The Biggest Variable

Rent will be 40–55% of most expat budgets. The range across neighborhoods is wide:

Key notes: furnished means TV, bed, basic kitchen appliances. Most short-term rentals (under 3 months) run on Airbnb at $800–1,500/month — negotiate directly with hosts for longer stays and you'll typically get 20–35% off. Unfurnished long-term leases (1+ year) run 20–30% below the figures above.

The Laureles/Envigado arbitrage: Most first-time expats default to El Poblado and pay $200–400/month more than necessary for equivalent quality. Laureles has better walkability scores, quieter streets, and apartments that are often newer — at 30–40% less rent. Many long-term Medellín residents end up in Laureles or Envigado after their first few months.

The menú del día is one of the best deals in the world: a three-course lunch (soup, protein + rice + salad, fresh juice) for under $4. Eating this daily for lunch cuts your food budget dramatically. Medellín's metro system is genuinely excellent and covers most of the city including the cable car lines to hillside comunas. Most expats in El Poblado, Laureles, and Envigado don't own a car — taxis and InDrive are cheap and traffic makes driving unpleasant. Monthly transport budget: $30–80 for most lifestyles.

What's Gotten More Expensive Since 2022

Medellín has gentrified rapidly. A few categories have seen meaningful price increases for expats:

  • El Poblado rent: Up 40–60% in USD terms since 2020 due to a surge of digital nomads and remote workers. Still cheap vs the US, but no longer "absurdly cheap."
  • Short-term rentals: Airbnb prices in El Poblado now rival some European cities at peak. Negotiate directly for stays over 30 days.
  • Imported goods: Anything imported (wine, cheese, some electronics) has gotten more expensive as the peso weakened then partially recovered.
  • Popular expat restaurants: The tourist-facing restaurant scene in El Poblado has converged toward $20–35/person dinner pricing — still cheaper than NYC but no longer budget.

What hasn't changed: local Colombian food, transport, healthcare, utilities, and domestic services remain extremely affordable relative to any developed-world baseline.

FAQ: Cost of Living in Medellín

How much does it cost to live in Medellín per month?

$1,200–1,800/month for a comfortable single-person lifestyle. Budget living is possible from $900/month. A premium lifestyle in El Poblado runs $2,800–3,800/month. Couples sharing expenses typically hit $1,500–2,200/month comfortably.

How much is rent in Medellín in 2026?

Furnished 1BR: El Poblado $600–1,100/month, Laureles $450–750/month, Envigado $400–650/month. Unfurnished long-term leases run 20–30% cheaper. Shared rooms from $250–450/month depending on neighborhood.

Is Medellín still cheap to live in?

Compared to the US, Canada, or Western Europe — very much yes. El Poblado specifically has gentrified and is no longer as cheap as it was in 2019–2021. Laureles and Envigado offer similar quality at 25–40% lower cost and are where most long-term expats end up.

What income do you need to live well in Medellín?

$1,500–2,000/month after tax puts most single people in a very comfortable position. $2,500+ and you're living exceptionally well by any global standard. Many long-term expats find $1,200–1,500/month is enough once they've settled into local habits and moved out of El Poblado.
