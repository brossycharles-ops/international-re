Argentina Real Estate for Foreigners 2026: The Complete Buying Guide

By International RE April 29, 2026 14 min read

Argentina is one of the most unusual real estate markets in the world. The peso has lost 98% of its value since 2001. Yet property transactions are conducted entirely in US dollars — meaning foreign buyers capture a real discount of 30–50% against 2018 prices while getting full legal ownership rights equivalent to any Argentine citizen.

There are no restrictions on foreign ownership. No minimum investment. No requirement to be a resident. You need a CDI (tax ID), a passport, USD in hand, and a good local attorney. That's it.

This guide covers everything: the legal process, the true costs, the best neighborhoods by strategy, the risks (capital controls, macroeconomic volatility), and how to structure a purchase correctly in 2026.

The core opportunity in one sentence: Buenos Aires apartments that traded at $2,000–3,000/sqm in 2018 now trade at $1,200–1,800/sqm in USD — with gross rental yields of 4–7% in dollars and a market Milei's reforms are gradually stabilizing.

Can Foreigners Own Property in Argentina?

Yes, fully. Argentina's Civil and Commercial Code grants foreign nationals identical ownership rights to citizens. You can own any property type: apartments, houses, commercial buildings, land, agricultural estancias. There is no cap on how much you can own, no foreign ownership tax, and no requirement to use the property in any specific way.

The one restriction worth knowing: the 2011 Foreign Land Law caps foreign ownership of rural land over 1,000 hectares in certain border zones and limits total foreign ownership of any given rural area to 15%. This rarely affects typical residential or urban commercial buyers.

What You Actually Need to Buy

The paperwork is minimal for what you get:

  • CDI (Clave de Identificación) — Argentina's tax ID for foreigners. Obtained at any AFIP (tax authority) office in one day with your passport. Free.
  • Valid passport — You don't need a visa, tourist entry is sufficient.
  • USD funds — Properties are priced and paid in US dollars. Wire transfers, physical cash (common), or cryptocurrency settlement (increasingly used) all work. Argentine pesos are not used in real estate.
  • Local attorney (escribano) — The escribano is Argentina's notary-attorney hybrid who verifies title, drafts contracts, and registers the deed. You need one. Budget $1,500–3,000.

No residency required. You can close a purchase as a tourist, fly home, and own the property legally. Many foreign buyers never live in Argentina at all.

The Step-by-Step Buying Process

  1. Get your CDI Visit any AFIP office with your passport. Takes 1–2 hours. No cost. You cannot sign a purchase contract without this.
  2. Find a property and negotiate Argentines negotiate hard — 10–20% off asking is common, especially for vacant apartments. Price in USD is non-negotiable; method of payment is.
  3. Sign the Boleto de Compraventa The preliminary purchase contract. Typically requires a 10–30% deposit. This document locks in the price and timeline. Your escribano must review this before you sign.
  4. Title and lien search (estudio de títulos) Your escribano runs a 20-year title chain search and checks for liens, debts (expensas owed to the building), and any encumbrances. Takes 15–30 days.
  5. Escritura (deed closing) Both parties sign the escritura before the escribano. Remaining balance is paid — most commonly in physical USD cash in an "escritura room" at the escribano's office. Wire transfers work but require BCRA (Central Bank) compliance filings.
  6. Registration The escribano registers the deed with the Registro de la Propiedad Inmueble. Takes 30–60 days. You own the property from the moment of signing, not registration.

Currency Controls and How Money Actually Moves

This is where Argentina gets complicated. The country has had foreign exchange controls (cepo cambiario) on and off since 2011. Under Milei, controls were significantly loosened in April 2025 — the official rate unified and the parallel dollar spread collapsed from 150% to under 20% by late 2025.

In practice, here's how foreign buyers move money into property:

  • Wire transfer (legal, BCRA-reported): Wire USD to an Argentine bank account. You receive USD at the official rate. For real estate purchases over $100K, this is increasingly straightforward under current BCRA rules.
  • Physical USD cash: Historically the most common method. You bring (or have an intermediary bring) physical $100 bills. No FX conversion. Settlement happens in the escribano's office. Still widely used in 2026.
  • USDT / stablecoin: Growing rapidly since 2022. Buyer sends USDT to seller's exchange account; seller converts to ARS or holds in USDT. Legally grey but widely practiced. Ask your attorney if this fits your situation.

Important: Always document your money's origin clearly — wire transfer receipts, bank statements showing USD leaving your foreign account. This matters for repatriation later. Argentina has an ITI foreign repatriation process and if you can't prove the source, you may face withholding on exit.

What Milei's Reforms Mean for Foreign Buyers

Javier Milei took office in December 2023 and immediately began dismantling Argentina's interventionist economic architecture. For foreign real estate buyers, the key changes:

  • Exchange rate unification (April 2025): The multiple exchange rates — official, MEP, blue — largely collapsed into one. Reduces FX arbitrage complexity but also eliminates the "blue dollar" premium that previously made Argentina even cheaper.
  • RIGI (Large Investment Incentive Regime): For investments over $200M in specific sectors (mining, energy, tech), creates a 30-year stability guarantee against tax and regulatory changes. Not relevant for typical residential buyers but signals commitment to rule of law.
  • Deregulation of rental market: The 2020 rental law that capped increases and locked in long lease terms was repealed. Landlord-tenant relationships are now more freely negotiated — good for investors.
  • Spending cuts and deficit elimination: Argentina ran a fiscal surplus for the first time in decades in 2024. This has reduced inflation from 300%+ (Dec 2023) toward 50–60% annualized (late 2025). Structural improvement but not stability yet.

The honest risk: Argentina has defaulted on sovereign debt 9 times. The reforms are real and meaningful but Milei faces political headwinds in 2026 midterm elections. A reversal of exchange rate policy or capital controls would complicate repatriation of rental income. This market rewards patience and risk tolerance — it is not a passive, set-and-forget investment.

FAQ: Argentina Real Estate for Foreigners

Can foreigners buy property in Argentina?

Yes, with the same rights as Argentine citizens. No visa, no residency, no minimum investment required. You need a CDI tax ID (obtained in one day) and USD funds.

How much cheaper is Argentine real estate in US dollars?

30–50% cheaper versus 2018 peaks in real USD terms. A Palermo apartment that cost $2,500/sqm in 2018 now trades at $1,400–1,800/sqm. Prices are denominated in USD, so you capture the discount directly without currency conversion risk.

Do I need to be a resident to buy property in Argentina?

No. You can purchase as a tourist with no visa. Hundreds of foreigners own Argentine property without ever having lived there. You need a CDI, passport, and USD.

What are the closing costs when buying in Argentina?

Budget 8–10% on top of purchase price: ~4% agent commission, 1.5–2% escribano, 2.5–3.5% stamp duty, ~0.5% registration. The stamp duty rate varies by province — Buenos Aires is 3.6%.

Can I get a mortgage as a foreigner in Argentina?

Practically no. Argentine mortgage markets are dysfunctional (mortgage credit is under 1% of GDP). Almost all transactions are cash. This is actually why the market is so cheap — no leveraged buyers. Plan to pay 100% cash.

Is Argentina Right for You?

Argentina suits buyers who: (1) have patient USD capital and can tolerate 5–10 year hold periods, (2) understand LatAm political risk and aren't scared off by it, (3) want high nominal yields on dollar-denominated assets, or (4) plan to spend meaningful time in Buenos Aires and want to own rather than rent.

It is not right for buyers who need: liquidity (selling takes 3–6 months), predictable policy environments, or mortgage leverage.

The setup that works: buy a 1–2 BR apartment in Palermo or Villa Crespo under $150K, furnish it, list on Airbnb targeting foreign visitors and digital nomads, collect 5–7% gross yield in USD, and hold for the eventual macro normalization that pushes prices back toward $2,000–2,500/sqm. The upside scenario is a 40–60% price recovery over 5 years plus yield along the way.
