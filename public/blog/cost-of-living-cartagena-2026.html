Cost of Living in Cartagena 2026: Real Expat Budgets

April 29, 2026 · 10 min read · By International RE Research

Cartagena is Colombia's most photogenic city — a UNESCO-heritage walled city of pastel colonial buildings, Caribbean beaches, and 500 years of history. It's also Colombia's most expensive city for expats, sitting roughly 20–35% above Medellín on most cost categories due to its established tourism economy.

For USD or EUR earners, Cartagena is still dramatically affordable by Western standards. Here's what it actually costs to live here in 2026, neighborhood by neighborhood.

Cartagena vs. Medellín in one line: Cartagena trades Medellín's eternal spring climate and lower costs for Caribbean beaches, the most beautiful old city in South America, and an established international expat/tourist infrastructure. It's the right tradeoff if beach access and colonial beauty matter more to you than budget optimization.

Food and Groceries

Cartagena's food scene divides into two tiers: the tourist-priced restaurants in Bocagrande and the walled city ($15–50 per meal), and the local market and neighborhood economy that most residents use daily. Cooking at home brings costs in line with the rest of Colombia.

The two-tier economy: Restaurants in the walled city and on Bocagrande's main strip charge 2–4× local prices. Locals and budget-conscious expats eat in Manga, El Cabrero, or at market stalls. One of the most important Cartagena money skills is knowing when to leave the tourist zone to eat.

No car needed in core Cartagena: Bocagrande, Getsemaní, and the walled city are all walkable to each other. InDriver and mototaxis cover everything else cheaply. A car only makes sense if you plan day trips to Barranquilla, Santa Marta, or the surrounding coast regularly.

Healthcare

Cartagena has solid private hospitals — Clínica Medihelp and Clínica Universitaria San Juan de Dios are the main options. For serious conditions, many expats travel to Bogotá or Medellín (1.5-hour flight). Legal residents must enroll in Colombia's EPS system.

AC is not optional in Cartagena: Average year-round temperature is 88°F with high humidity. Electricity bills are the highest variable cost in Cartagena vs. other Colombian cities — budget $60–120/month for a 1BR apartment running AC at night. Rooftop apartments and ocean-facing units can use sea breezes to reduce AC dependency somewhat.

How much does it cost to live in Cartagena per month?

A single expat can live comfortably in Cartagena for $1,500–2,500/month in 2026. Budget living in Getsemaní or Manga starts around $1,000/month. A couple lives comfortably for $2,000–3,500/month in Bocagrande. Premium living in El Laguito or Castillogrande runs $3,000–4,500/month for a couple. Cartagena is 20–35% more expensive than Medellín on comparable lifestyle.

How much is rent in Cartagena in 2026?

Furnished 1BR apartments: El Cabrero/Crespo $350–650/month, Getsemaní $400–700/month, Manga $500–850/month, Bocagrande $700–1,300/month, El Laguito $900–1,600/month, walled city $800–1,800/month. Long-term leases (6+ months) cut costs 15–25% vs. short-term rates. Most expats find the best value in Bocagrande for beach access or Getsemaní for culture.

Is Cartagena more expensive than Medellín?

Yes — Cartagena runs 20–35% higher than Medellín for comparable accommodation and restaurant dining. The tourism economy inflates prices in the walled city and Bocagrande specifically. Grocery costs at local markets are competitive. For cost optimization, Medellín wins clearly. For Caribbean beach access and colonial beauty, Cartagena's premium is considered worthwhile by most expats who choose it.

Is Cartagena safe for expats in 2026?

Yes, in the main expat areas. Bocagrande, El Laguito, Castillogrande, and the gentrified part of Getsemaní all have good safety records. The walled city tourist zone is well-patrolled. Use InDriver or Cabify rather than street taxis at night, don't display valuables, and avoid isolated areas after dark. Cartagena is safer than Bogotá and generally safe for expats exercising standard urban precautions.
