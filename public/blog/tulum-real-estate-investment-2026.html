Tulum Real Estate Investment 2026: Yields, Risks & What to Actually Buy

By International RE April 29, 2026 14 min read

Tulum has been the loudest real estate story in Mexico for five years. The pitch writes itself: eco-luxury aesthetic, surging global demand, new international airport, Maya Train connectivity, Airbnb yields of 10%+. And the data largely backs it up — prices have appreciated 40–60% since 2020, and well-managed short-term rentals consistently generate gross yields of 8–14%.

But Tulum in 2026 is not Tulum in 2020. There are now thousands of condos either delivered, under construction, or in pre-sale. Infrastructure has not kept pace. Developer failures have burned buyers. The easy-money arbitrage window has closed. The question isn't whether Tulum works — it does, for the right buyer. The question is whether your specific deal works.

This guide gives you the honest version: what the yields actually look like net of all costs, which zones perform, how to vet developers, and the red flags that separate the 10% of deals worth doing from the 90% that look good on a developer slide deck.

The Tulum thesis in one sentence: High gross yields (8–14%) and appreciation upside are real, but net yields of 5–9% after all costs, a supply surge, and developer risk require active management and careful underwriting — this is not a passive investment.

The Yield Reality: Gross vs. Net

Developer presentations always quote gross yield. Net yield — what you actually receive — is dramatically different:

Gross annual rental revenue100% (baseline)

HOA / maintenance fee−5 to −10%

Vacancy (low/shoulder season)−8 to −15%
Net yield (estimated range)~35–50% of gross revenue

In concrete terms: a condo generating $30,000/year gross STR revenue (10% gross yield on a $300K property) nets approximately $10,500–15,000 after all costs — a 3.5–5% net yield. A property generating $40,000/year gross (13% gross) might net $14,000–20,000 — a 4.7–6.7% net yield. Still respectable. Not life-changing.

Developer "guaranteed yield" programs: Many Tulum developers offer 8–12% "guaranteed" annual returns for 1–3 years. These are typically prepaid from your own purchase price (the developer marks up the unit, puts the markup in escrow, pays it back as "yield"). After the guarantee period, you're on your own — and often holding an overpriced asset. Treat any guaranteed yield as a red flag requiring deep scrutiny.

Zones: Where Performance Actually Differs

Aldea Zamá

$2,800–5,000/sqm
Net Yield: 5–8%

Master-planned community north of the Hotel Zone. Best infrastructure in Tulum — paved roads, reliable electricity, water treatment. Highest sustained occupancy. Most competitive and most expensive established zone. Best for capital preservation + yield.

La Veleta

$2,200–4,000/sqm
Net Yield: 5–9%

Immediately west of the Hotel Zone. Urban amenities (supermarkets, gyms, restaurants) within walking distance. More density than Aldea Zamá but better infrastructure than jungle zones. Strong digital nomad long-term rental market alongside STR.

Tulum Hotel Zone (Zona Hotelera)

$4,000–12,000+/sqm
Net Yield: 4–7%

Beachfront strip with maximum ADR potential ($250–600/night) but also maximum price. Infrastructure constraints are worst here — CENGAS gas reliability, water truck dependency. Lower net yields than inland zones despite higher gross revenue.

Jungle / Cenote Zones

$1,800–3,500/sqm
Net Yield: 3–7% (highly variable)

Lower entry prices but infrastructure is the real risk: dirt roads flood in rain season, electricity outages, limited water. Stunning natural setting drives ADR, but management intensity and maintenance costs are highest. High variance outcomes.

Tulum Pueblo (Town)

$1,500–2,800/sqm
LTR Yield: 5–7%

Urban core 2km inland from the beach. Growing digital nomad and long-term rental market. Better infrastructure than jungle zones, cheaper entry than Aldea Zamá. Less Instagram-worthy — lower ADR ceiling but more stable year-round occupancy.

Tulum Outskirts / New Developments

$1,200–2,200/sqm
Net Yield: Unknown / High Risk

Speculative pre-construction far from established zones. Lowest entry prices, highest developer risk, no track record. The zone where most developer failures have occurred. Avoid unless you have exceptional due diligence capability.

Developer Vetting: The Non-Negotiables

Developer risk is the single biggest threat to Tulum investments. At least a dozen notable developer failures have occurred since 2021 — projects that took deposits, stopped construction, and left buyers with unfinished shells or total losses. Here is the minimum due diligence required before committing any funds:

  1. Completed projects: The developer must have delivered at least 2–3 completed projects in Tulum or the Riviera Maya. Ask for addresses. Visit them. Talk to owners.
  2. Fideicomiso (trust) in place: For Restricted Zone properties, the fideicomiso should be established with a major bank (BBVA, Banamex, Scotiabank) before or at the time of your deposit. Never wire money to a developer's personal or operating account.
  3. Building permit (licencia de construcción): Demand to see the actual permit before signing anything. Projects frequently sell pre-construction before permits are approved — this is legal in Mexico but a significant risk factor.
  4. Environmental permit (MIA/ETJ): Tulum's jungle proximity makes environmental compliance critical. Projects without a Manifestación de Impacto Ambiental approved by SEMARNAT face shutdown risk.
  5. Escrow for deposits: Your deposit (10–30% of purchase price) should go into a third-party escrow account, not to the developer directly. Reputable developers accept this. Walk away from any developer who refuses.
  6. Delivery timeline with liquidated damages: The purchase contract must specify a delivery date and a penalty clause (daños y perjuicios) if the developer misses it. Without penalties, the timeline is meaningless.

The Supply Pipeline: What It Means for Your Return

As of early 2026, Tulum has an estimated 6,000–8,000 STR units in operation and another 4,000–6,000 units in various stages of construction or pre-sale. This is a massive supply increase for a market that had under 3,000 units in 2020.

The implications:

  • Occupancy compression: Average occupancy in established zones has declined from ~75% (2022) to ~65–70% (2025) as supply has grown faster than demand. Properties with weak management or poor location are seeing 55–60% occupancy.
  • ADR resilience: Average daily rates have held up better — $180–280/night in established zones — because Tulum's brand continues to attract premium travelers. Rate compression is modest so far.
  • Management quality matters more: In a high-supply environment, listing optimization, professional photography, responsive hosting, and dynamic pricing separate properties in the top quartile (80%+ occupancy) from the median. Self-managing from abroad is increasingly non-viable.

What to Buy vs. What to Avoid

✓ Worth Considering

  • Completed units in Aldea Zamá or La Veleta from proven developer
  • Properties with 12+ months of verified STR revenue history
  • Buildings with onsite management company already operating
  • Unique design/amenity that commands premium ADR (rooftop pool, cenote access)
  • Pre-construction from developer with 3+ delivered projects and escrow in place
  • Tulum Pueblo long-term rental plays for digital nomad demand

✗ Red Flags to Avoid

  • Guaranteed yield programs (8–12% "guaranteed") from developers
  • Pre-construction without building permit already approved
  • Jungle/remote zones with no infrastructure track record
  • Developers with no completed Tulum projects
  • Deposits wired directly to developer (no escrow)
  • No fideicomiso in place for Restricted Zone property
  • Projected yields based on peak-season-only occupancy

FAQ: Tulum Real Estate Investment

Is Tulum real estate a good investment in 2026?

For active investors with strong due diligence and professional management: yes. Gross yields of 8–14% and 40%+ appreciation since 2020 are real. For passive investors expecting developer-guaranteed returns: no. Net yields of 5–9% after all costs are solid but not exceptional, and developer risk requires careful vetting.

What are average rental yields in Tulum?

Gross yields on established-zone condos: 8–14%. Net yields after management (30%), platform fees, HOA, vacancy, and maintenance: 5–9%. Budget 50% of gross revenue for all expenses as a conservative underwriting assumption.

How much does a condo cost in Tulum in 2026?

Studio/1BR in Aldea Zamá or La Veleta: $120,000–280,000. Mid-range 1–2BR with amenities: $200,000–400,000. Beachfront Hotel Zone: $400,000–1,200,000+. Pre-construction pricing runs 15–25% below completed-unit pricing but carries delivery risk.

What is the biggest risk in Tulum real estate?

Developer failure on pre-construction projects. At least a dozen notable failures since 2021 have left buyers with unfinished projects or total losses. Always use escrow for deposits, verify existing completed projects, and demand a building permit before signing anything.
