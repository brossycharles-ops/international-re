Costa Rica Digital Nomad Visa 2026: Requirements, Cost & How to Apply

By International RE  ·  April 30, 2026  ·  10 min read

Costa Rica launched its Digital Nomad Visa in 2022 under Law 9996, becoming one of the first Central American countries to formally recognize remote workers as a distinct immigration category. In 2026, the visa is well-established, the processing pathway is clear, and tens of thousands of remote workers have gone through it. This guide covers the exact requirements, the full document checklist, the application sequence, the tax implications, and an honest assessment of what life looks like on the ground.

The Core Requirements

Costa Rica's Digital Nomad Visa (officially Categoría Especial: Nómada Digital) has three primary requirements:

  • Income threshold: $3,000/month minimum from sources outside Costa Rica. With a spouse/partner: $3,000 + $500 = $3,500/month. With one child: +$500 more. Income can be salary from a foreign employer, freelance/contractor income, business income, dividends, or investment returns — as long as it does not come from Costa Rican clients or the Costa Rican market.
  • Remote work nature: You must be working remotely for a company or clients based outside Costa Rica, or running a business whose revenue comes from outside the country.
  • Health insurance: Valid health insurance covering the full duration of your stay in Costa Rica. Either international health insurance or enrollment in CAJA (Costa Rica's public system) qualifies.

There is no minimum savings requirement, no age restriction, and no language requirement. The visa does not require you to invest in Costa Rica or purchase property — it is purely income and remote work based.

Full Document Checklist

Documents Required for Application

  • Valid passport (minimum 1 year validity remaining)
  • Completed application form (available at Costa Rican consulate or DGME website)
  • Proof of income — bank statements (last 3–6 months), employment contract, or accountant letter showing $3,000+/month from non-CR sources
  • If employed: letter from employer confirming remote work status and monthly salary, on company letterhead
  • If self-employed/freelance: proof of contracts or invoices + accountant certification of income
  • Background check from your home country (apostilled)
  • Proof of health insurance valid in Costa Rica
  • 2 passport-size photos
  • Birth certificates for dependents (apostilled)
  • Marriage certificate if including spouse (apostilled)
  • All documents in Spanish or with certified Spanish translation

Apostille note: US applicants get apostilles from the Secretary of State in their home state for most documents, or from the US Department of State for federal documents (FBI background check). Processing takes 2–6 weeks. Start this first — it's often the longest lead-time item.

The Application Process Step by Step

  1. Enter Costa Rica on a tourist visa (90 days, no visa needed for US/EU/UK/CA)

    Most applicants apply in-country rather than at a consulate abroad. You can enter as a tourist and submit your application to the DGME (Dirección General de Migración y Extranjería) in San José while legally in the country.

  2. Gather and apostille your documents (start immediately)

    Request your FBI background check, gather 3–6 months of bank statements, get your employer letter, and begin the apostille process. Budget 4–8 weeks for US FBI apostille processing.

  3. Get certified Spanish translations

    All foreign documents must be translated by a certified Costa Rican translator (traductor oficial). Cost: $30–$80 per document. There are many translators in San José; your immigration attorney will typically handle this.

  4. Hire a local immigration attorney (recommended, not mandatory)

    Not legally required, but strongly recommended for first-time applicants. A Costa Rican immigration attorney reviews your file before submission, catches missing items, and monitors the case. Cost: $500–$1,500. Well worth it to avoid a rejection that adds months to the process.

  5. Submit to DGME and pay government fee (~$250)

    Submit in person at the DGME office in San José (La Uruca). You'll receive a receipt and a case number. Your status changes to "in process" — you can continue living in Costa Rica legally while waiting.

  6. Wait 2–4 months for processing

    The DGME processes applications in order. During this time, your tourism status is effectively extended — you will not accrue overstay violations while your application is pending. Your attorney monitors the case online.

  7. Receive approval and collect your DIMEX card

    Upon approval, you collect your DIMEX (cédula de identidad) residency card at the DGME. This card is your legal ID in Costa Rica and enables you to open bank accounts, sign leases, and access services.

Tax Implications: What You Actually Owe

This is where nomads get confused. Clear breakdown:

Costa Rican taxes: Zero. The Digital Nomad Visa explicitly exempts holders from Costa Rican income tax on income from foreign sources. You are not a Costa Rican tax resident under this visa category, and your foreign income is not taxable in Costa Rica.

Your home country taxes: This depends entirely on your nationality and tax residency situation. Key cases:

  • US citizens: The US taxes worldwide income regardless of where you live. However, the Foreign Earned Income Exclusion (FEIE) allows you to exclude up to $126,500/year (2024 figure, indexed annually) of foreign-earned income if you meet the Physical Presence Test (330+ days outside the US in a 12-month period). The Foreign Tax Credit (FTC) can offset any residual US liability. Many US nomads in CR owe little or no net US tax, but you must file. Work with a cross-border CPA.
  • EU citizens: Tax rules vary by country. Most EU countries use residency-based taxation — if you're no longer a tax resident in your home country, you generally don't owe them tax. Requirements for exiting tax residency differ (Germany and France are strict; Estonia and Portugal have specific nomad schemes). Consult a tax advisor in your specific country.
  • Canadian citizens: Canada also taxes worldwide income for tax residents. "Factual residence" rules mean you can continue to owe Canadian tax even while abroad. Most nomads who leave Canada for 2+ years and sever ties (sell property, end provincial health coverage, close local accounts) can become non-resident for tax purposes. Seek Canadian tax advice before departing.

The FEIE Physical Presence Test in Practice

To claim the FEIE, you need 330+ days outside the US in any 12-month period (not calendar year). Most nomads in Costa Rica on a 1-year visa easily meet this. Days spent traveling through the US or other countries count as "outside" as long as the cumulative US days stay under 35. Keep a travel log — the IRS requires documentation on Form 2555. With careful planning, most US nomads earning under $126,500/year in earned income owe zero net US income tax on that income while abroad.

Best Locations for Nomads in Costa Rica

San José (Escazú / Santa Ana)

The practical base for most new arrivals — best infrastructure, fastest internet (fiber widely available, 100–500 Mbps), most co-working spaces, easiest access to the DGME for visa processing. Not glamorous, but functional. Bolt access to the airport, hospitals, and shopping. Monthly rent for a furnished 1BR: $900–$1,400.

Tamarindo / Nosara (Guanacaste)

The beach nomad experience. Tamarindo has strong co-working infrastructure, good fiber internet in the town center, an international community, and direct flights to the US via Liberia airport (LIR). Nosara is more secluded — surf-focused, yoga-heavy, excellent internet in the surf zones, higher prices. Monthly rent: $1,000–$1,800 for a 1BR.

Santa Teresa

The most photogenic but least practical option. Beautiful, slow, excellent surf. Internet has improved significantly (fiber in parts of town) but power outages still occur. Roads require 4WD. Best for nomads who prioritize lifestyle over logistics and don't have video call-heavy work. Monthly rent: $1,200–$2,000.

Puerto Viejo (Caribbean Coast)

Costa Rica's Caribbean side — reggae culture, Afro-Caribbean food, calmer sea, jungle. Much cheaper than the Pacific coast ($500–$900/month rent). Slower pace, smaller nomad community. Internet is adequate in town but not reliable for heavy video work. Best for long-stay writers, creators, or location-independent workers without intensive connectivity needs.
