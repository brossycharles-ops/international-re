How to Buy Property in Costa Rica as a Foreigner (2026 Guide)

April 29, 2026  ·  14 min read  ·  International RE

The short answer: foreigners can buy property in Costa Rica freely, easily, and with the same rights as Costa Rican citizens. There is no foreign ownership restriction, no quota, no requirement to be a resident. But there are a few traps — the maritime zone law being the biggest — and the legal process has specific steps worth understanding before you wire any money.

This guide covers the complete process: legal rights, how title works, what the maritime zone means for beach properties, closing costs, the typical timeline, what to watch for, and where prices actually stand in 2026.

1. Foreign Property Rights in Costa Rica

Under Article 19 of the Costa Rican Constitution and the Aliens Law, foreigners have identical property rights to Costa Rican citizens. This is unusually strong compared to many countries — Mexico, Thailand, Cambodia, Indonesia, and Vietnam all have significant restrictions on foreign ownership that Costa Rica simply does not have.

Key points:

  • No nationality restrictions. Citizens of any country can purchase.
  • No residency requirement. You can buy as a tourist.
  • No cap on the percentage of a property a foreigner can own.
  • No special permits or government approvals required for standard transactions.
  • You can hold property in your own name or through a Costa Rican corporation (S.A. or S.R.L.).

The exception — and it's a significant one — is beach and waterfront property subject to the Maritime Zone Law.

2. The Maritime Zone Trap

This is the single most common source of costly mistakes for foreign buyers in Costa Rica. Understanding it clearly before you look at beach properties is essential.

The Ley de la Zona Marítimo Terrestre (ZMT) divides the coastal strip into two zones:

The Two Coastal Zones

Public Zone (Zona Pública) — 0 to 50 meters from high tide: This land is entirely public. It cannot be owned by anyone, bought, sold, or titled. Period. Any "sale" of land in the public zone is legally meaningless. Do not buy anything in this strip.

Restricted Zone (Zona Restringida) — 50 to 200 meters from high tide: This land can only be held via a concession granted by the local municipality. The concession is a lease, not ownership. And here's the critical part: only Costa Rican citizens or companies with at least 50% Costa Rican ownership can hold concessions. A foreigner cannot hold a concession directly.

What this means in practice:

  • Many beachfront and near-beach properties in Costa Rica are concession land, not titled land.
  • A foreigner "buying" a concession property is actually buying a company that holds the concession — which means you need a company with at least 50% Costa Rican ownership on paper, which creates obvious risks.
  • Some municipalities have created special zones (like Tamarindo and parts of Nosara) where development rights work differently — get a lawyer who specializes in this area before assuming.
  • Properties outside the 200-meter restricted zone are standard titled land. This is where most legitimate foreign investment happens.

Bottom line: Always get a Costa Rican property attorney to confirm the exact legal status of any coastal property before signing anything. The fee (typically $1,500–3,000) is trivial compared to the risk.

3. Step-by-Step Buying Process

Step 1: Engage a local attorney (abogado)

Costa Rica uses a notarial system — a notary-attorney (notario público) must handle the transfer deed. You need your own attorney, not just the seller's. Budget $1,500–3,000 for a full-service property purchase. Get referrals from the expat community or established relocation consultants in the area you're buying.

Step 2: Title search (estudio de título)

Your attorney runs a full search through the Registro Nacional (National Registry) to confirm:

  • The seller is the legal owner
  • There are no liens, mortgages, or encumbrances on the property
  • Property taxes are current (check the Municipalidad records)
  • The property is not in a flood zone, protected area, or maritime zone
This takes 3–7 business days.

Step 3: Survey (plano catastrado)

If one doesn't exist or is outdated, you'll need a licensed topographer to survey the property and confirm the physical boundaries match the registered title. Cost: $500–1,500 depending on property size. Required for mortgage applications and highly recommended regardless.

Step 4: Due diligence and negotiation

Once title is confirmed clean, negotiate terms and put them in a Promesa de Compraventa (purchase agreement / letter of intent). This document outlines price, deposit (typically 10%), contingencies, and closing timeline. This is a binding contract once signed — have your attorney review it before you sign.

Step 5: Closing (escritura pública)

The notary-attorney prepares the transfer deed (escritura). Both parties (or their authorized representatives via power of attorney) sign before the notary. The deed is then submitted to the National Registry for recording.

Step 6: National Registry recording

After submission, the Registry records the transfer and updates the title to the new owner. This takes 2–4 weeks. Until this is complete, you own the property but it isn't yet reflected in the public record — your notary holds the original deed.

Step 7: Utility and municipal transfers

Once title is in your name, transfer utilities (ESPH, AyA, CNFL depending on location), update property tax records at the Municipalidad, and if applicable, set up the HOA account.

4. Closing Costs and Taxes

Total closing costs in Costa Rica typically run 3.5–5% of the purchase price, split between buyer and seller. The split is negotiable but conventionally about equal.

Annual property taxes: Costa Rica property tax (impuesto sobre bienes inmuebles) is 0.25% of the registered property value — one of the lowest in the world. Properties registered at market value still cost very little to hold annually.

7. Frequently Asked Questions

Can foreigners buy property in Costa Rica?

Yes. Foreigners have identical property rights to Costa Rican citizens. No residency required, no nationality restrictions, no ownership caps.

Do I need to form a company to buy property?

Not for titled inland or non-maritime property. Many buyers use a Costa Rican S.A. or S.R.L. for liability protection and ease of future sale, but it's optional. For concession properties near the beach, a company structure with Costa Rican co-ownership may be required.

Can I get a mortgage as a foreigner?

Costa Rican banks (Banco Nacional, BCR, BAC) do offer mortgages to foreigners, but requirements are strict: you typically need residency, local income, and a credit history in Costa Rica. Most foreign buyers purchase cash. Seller financing is common on private sales.

Is title insurance necessary?

Strongly recommended, especially for older properties with complex ownership histories. Stewart Title and First American Title both operate in Costa Rica. Cost is typically 0.3–0.5% of the property value — well worth it.

What taxes do I pay when I sell?

Capital gains tax is 15% on the gain if the property was purchased after July 2019. Properties purchased before that date are treated under the previous regime (2.25% of the sale price as a transfer tax, regardless of gain). Consult a Costa Rican tax attorney if selling an older property.
