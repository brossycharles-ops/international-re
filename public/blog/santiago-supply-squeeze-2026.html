Santiago's Housing Shortage Is the Best News for Real Estate Investors in 2026

By Sofia Mendez April 17, 2026 7 min read

In most real estate markets, falling construction permits are a warning sign. In Santiago, they're an investor's green light. Building permits in Greater Santiago dropped 25.4% year-over-year in 2025, to just 19,089 units — while the city's population and rental demand kept growing. The result: a structural supply squeeze that is pushing rents up, vacancies down, and valuations higher across the metro's most desirable corridors.

The Numbers Behind the Squeeze

Start with the price floor. Santiago's citywide average for apartments sits at roughly $2,494 per square meter as of Q1 2026, according to Global Property Guide — modest by global capital standards. But the neighborhoods attracting foreign buyers tell a different story:

  • Las Condes: $3,500–$4,500/sqm. The city's most liquid premium market, anchored by multinational corporate tenants and upper-middle-class Chilean families.
  • Providencia: $3,000–$3,800/sqm. Walkable, transit-connected, and dominated by apartments — the engine of Santiago's rental economy.
  • Vitacura: $4,000–$5,200/sqm. Santiago's highest-end enclave, where house prices command a premium and foreign buyers cluster.

Residential property prices rose 8.1% year-over-year nationally in Q3 2025, and analysts are forecasting another 3–7% for 2026. With supply constrained and demand steady, those forecasts are conservative.

Rental Yields: Not Spectacular, But Reliable

Chilean yields are not Guanacaste. You're not running 10% vacation-rental numbers here. But what you get in Santiago is something different: consistency.

Gross yields citywide run 4.6–4.8%, with small apartments in high-demand micro-markets reaching 5–6.5%. Vacancy for studio and one-bedroom units sits near 2% — essentially full occupancy. Rents are growing 3–6% year-over-year as the supply pipeline dries up.

Antofagasta, Chile's northern mining hub, is worth a mention: gross yields there hit 6.1–6.2%, the highest in the country. But for foreign buyers prioritizing liquidity and resale depth, Santiago remains the cleaner bet.

The Metro Catalyst

Here is where Santiago's story gets genuinely interesting. The city is building two major metro extensions simultaneously:

  • Línea 7: A 26-kilometer line threading through Renca, Quinta Normal, Providencia, and Las Condes. Corridors adjacent to future stations are already seeing demand pull-forward from buyers anticipating the connectivity premium.
  • Línea 9: Expanding coverage to additional underserved neighborhoods, opening new rental catchment zones.

Transit-adjacent property in growing cities is one of the most repeatable capital appreciation plays in real estate. When a metro line opens, property values within a 500-meter radius typically jump 8–15% within three years. Santiago's Línea 7 corridor is years from completion — which means buyers who move now are buying ahead of that repricing.

Why the Macro Tailwinds Are Real

Chile's central bank cut its policy rate to 4.5% by December 2025, down from an 11.25% peak in 2023. That's a dramatic improvement in domestic mortgage affordability — and it has pulled Chilean buyers back into the market, increasing competition for the limited existing inventory that foreign cash buyers are also targeting.

The peso has strengthened roughly 7% against the USD over the past year, driven by high copper prices (Chile produces roughly a third of the world's supply). USD buyers are getting somewhat less purchasing power than in 2023. But analysts expect the USD/CLP rate to stabilize in the 880–940 range, and a copper pullback — whenever it comes — would briefly reopen a more favorable entry window.

The broader macro picture remains stable: Chile maintains investment-grade sovereign credit ratings, a functioning rule of law, and no restrictions on capital repatriation. These aren't glamorous advantages, but they matter enormously when you're managing an asset from overseas.

The Foreign Buyer Advantage

Chile is one of the most straightforward countries in Latin America for foreign ownership. There are no quotas, no approval processes, no local-partner requirements. Foreigners hold identical property rights to Chilean citizens for residential real estate.

You'll need a Chilean RUT (tax ID), which a local notary can help obtain. Closing costs run 3–5% of the purchase price. Annual property taxes are modest. And critically: Chilean banks generally do not extend mortgages to non-residents, so virtually all foreign transactions are cash. That's actually a filter that protects prices — only serious buyers participate.

Where to Focus

Given the data, three plays stand out for 2026:

  1. Small apartments in Providencia and Las Condes. The best combination of yield (5–6.5% gross on studios/1-beds), vacancy (near zero), and liquidity. These neighborhoods also benefit most from the Línea 7 extension.
  2. Transit-corridor acquisitions near future Línea 7 stations. Buy the infrastructure story now, before the completion premium lands. Target properties within 500 meters of announced station locations in Providencia and Las Condes.
  3. Vitacura for capital preservation. Lower yields, but maximum resale depth and the city's strongest price floor. For buyers more focused on value storage than income, this is Santiago's equivalent of a core gateway asset.

The Bottom Line

Santiago is not a yield story. It's a stability-plus-appreciation story — underpinned by a supply collapse, a major transit buildout, and a macro environment that has shifted firmly in favor of landlords. For investors who want Latin American real estate exposure without the political noise of neighboring markets, Chile remains the region's most dependable entry point.

The permits aren't coming back soon. The metro is being built. The central bank is cutting. The window to buy ahead of the repricing is open — but it won't stay open indefinitely.

Sofia Mendez

Latin America real estate analyst covering Costa Rica and Chile. Based between San Jose and Santiago.
