Dominican Republic Real Estate 2026: Punta Cana, Las Terrenas & Cabarete

By International RE  ·  April 30, 2026  ·  13 min read

The Dominican Republic doesn't get the analytical respect it deserves. Investors fixate on Panama, Costa Rica, and Medellín — and the DR gets lumped in with "Caribbean vacation property." That's a mistake. In 2026, the DR offers the highest short-term rental yields of any beach market in the Western Hemisphere, zero property tax on purchases under $150,000, unrestricted foreign ownership, and a tourism juggernaut that posted 10.3 million arrivals in 2025 — more than Costa Rica and Panama combined.

The risks are real. Title issues exist in older rural transactions. Infrastructure outside the resort zones is uneven. The peso weakens against the dollar (though most transactions and rents are denominated in USD). This guide is not a brochure — it's a full breakdown of where the opportunity is, where the risk sits, and what a serious buyer needs to know before wiring money.

The Macro Picture: Why the DR Works for Investors

Three structural advantages make the Dominican Republic uniquely attractive among Caribbean real estate markets:

Tourism scale. The DR receives more international tourists than any other Caribbean nation by a wide margin. Punta Cana alone has 50+ all-inclusive resorts, direct flights from 50+ countries, and year-round occupancy that insulates the rental market from seasonal swings. This isn't a niche market — it's the largest tourism infrastructure in the Caribbean.

The property tax structure. The DR's IPI (Impuesto a la Propiedad Inmobiliaria) exempts all properties assessed below DOP 9.3 million (~$150,000 USD) from annual tax entirely. Above that threshold, the rate is 1% on the assessed value exceeding the threshold — and assessed values are typically below market value. A $200,000 condo might carry an annual tax bill of $500–$1,000. By comparison, a similar property in Florida pays $3,000–$6,000/year in property taxes.

USD transaction norm. Despite having a local currency (the Dominican peso), almost all real estate transactions, rental listings, and developer pricing are quoted in US dollars. This doesn't eliminate FX risk on local costs, but it means you buy, sell, and collect rent in dollars — currency risk on your returns is minimal compared to peso-denominated markets like Colombia or Argentina.

The Tax Advantage in Detail

The DR's tax structure is one of the most investor-friendly in the Americas:

  • Annual property tax (IPI): 0% on assessed value under ~$150,000. 1%/year on assessed value above the threshold — and assessed values run 20–40% below market. A $250,000 condo with a $180,000 assessed value pays 1% × $30,000 = $300/year.
  • Capital gains tax: 27% on gains — but only on the gain above the assessed value, which is typically lower than the sale price. In practice, many sellers structure transactions to minimize taxable gain. Consult a local attorney on the specifics for your situation.
  • Rental income tax: Rental income is taxable in the DR at 27% corporate rate if operating through a company, or ~25% individual income tax rate. Most investors use a local corporation (SRL) to hold property, which can deduct management fees, depreciation, and operating costs — reducing taxable income substantially.
  • Transfer tax: 3% of purchase price, paid at closing. This is the primary upfront tax on acquisition.
  • No inheritance tax: Unlike many European countries, the DR does not levy inheritance tax on real property — an important consideration for estate planning.

The CONFOTUR Tourism Incentive Law

Properties in CONFOTUR-registered developments receive a 10-year exemption from property transfer tax, annual property tax, and income tax on rental income. Most new-build resort developments in Punta Cana and Cap Cana carry CONFOTUR certification. When buying a pre-construction or recently completed condo in a resort zone, verify CONFOTUR status — it can save $5,000–$15,000 over 10 years on a $200,000 purchase. The exemption transfers to the new buyer on resale within the 10-year window.

Residency Visas for Investors and Retirees

The Dominican Republic offers several residency pathways for foreign property owners:

  • Investor Residency: Requires a minimum $200,000 investment in DR real estate or a business. Processed in 3–6 months. Leads to permanent residency after 2 years, citizenship eligibility after 5 years.
  • Rentista Visa: Passive income of $1,500/month (investment returns, rental income, dividends). A $200,000 property generating 9% yield ($18,000/year = $1,500/month) qualifies automatically.
  • Retirement Residency: $1,500/month in pension income. Same threshold as the Rentista. Most US retirees on Social Security + a small pension qualify.
  • Property owner residency (smaller pathway): Ownership of property valued at $150,000+ can support a residency application, though this is a supporting document rather than a standalone qualification.

The DR is one of the few countries in the Americas that allows naturalization after just 2 years of legal permanent residency (standard path) — though this requires renouncing any previous citizenship for many nationalities. Consult an immigration attorney on the specifics.

The Buying Process

The Dominican Republic uses a Torrens title system (Certificado de Título) for registered land. Titled property is safe to buy. The risk lies in older rural land parcels that may still be in the informal "constancia" system — these should be avoided by foreign buyers without experienced local counsel.

  1. Hire a local attorney before making an offer. Attorney fees run 1–1.5% of purchase price. This is non-negotiable — title verification is critical.
  2. Title search: Your attorney verifies the Certificado de Título at the Registro de Títulos. Confirms no liens, encumbrances, or disputes.
  3. Promise of sale (Promesa de Venta): Binding contract. Typically 10% deposit at signing.
  4. Closing: Balance paid. 3% transfer tax paid. New title issued in buyer's name (or company name). Process: 30–60 days.
  5. Closing costs total: 3% transfer tax + 1–1.5% attorney + ~0.5% registration fees = approximately 4.5–5% of purchase price.

Honest Risks: What Can Go Wrong

Title risk on older or rural property. Always verify you're buying a deslinde (titled) property at the Registro de Títulos. Rural land and older transactions may have "solar" or "constancia anotada" status — informal pre-title documentation. Avoid these unless you have an experienced local attorney who specializes in DR title regularization. Stick to titled condos from established developers for your first purchase.

  • Construction quality variance: Condo quality varies enormously between developers. Major developers (Grupo Puntacana, Cap Cana, Blue Mall, Cana Bay) have strong track records. Off-brand pre-construction projects have had delivery failures. Vet the developer's completed project portfolio before committing to pre-construction.
  • HOA fees: Resort condo HOAs in Punta Cana run $200–$600/month and include pool, security, grounds. Verify the HOA's financial health and reserve fund — underfunded HOAs are a risk in any market.
  • Property management: Airbnb management in resort zones is well-developed — multiple companies offer full-service management at 20–30% of revenue. The best outcomes come from using a local manager with an established booking pipeline, not a new entrant.
  • Peso inflation on operating costs: While rents are USD-denominated, local operating costs (staff, utilities, maintenance) are peso-denominated. Peso inflation (historically 5–8%/year) gradually erodes net margins on peso-cost items. Model this into your projections.
  • Infrastructure outside resort zones: Power cuts, water pressure issues, and road quality vary sharply outside the resort corridors. Many developments have generator backup and cisterns, but verify before buying.

The Bottom Line

The Dominican Republic is the most underdiscussed high-yield market in the Americas. When you can buy a condo producing 10%+ gross yield, pay zero annual property tax, receive a 10-year tax exemption through CONFOTUR, collect rent in dollars, and qualify for residency on the proceeds — that's a compelling structural case.

The risks are manageable with basic due diligence: buy titled property, use a reputable attorney, buy from established developers, and verify CONFOTUR certification on new builds. The mistakes that hurt buyers in the DR are almost always avoidable — they come from skipping the attorney, buying from unknown developers, or purchasing rural land without proper title verification.
