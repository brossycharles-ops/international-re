San Juan del Sur: Central America's Last Affordable Beach Town

By Carolina Vega March 24, 2026 7 min read

Twenty years ago, Tamarindo was a sleepy fishing village. Today, a beachfront condo there costs $400,000+. Ten years ago, Tulum was a backpacker secret. Now it's $5,000/sqm. The pattern is clear — and San Juan del Sur is next. But the prices haven't caught up yet.

The Price Gap Is Real

Nicaragua sits roughly 40–60% below Costa Rica on almost every real estate metric. Here's what that looks like in practice:

  • Entry-level lots: Starting at $21,000 in developments like Surf Ranch Popoyo
  • Beachfront lots: $80,000–$235,000 at Playa Popoyo
  • Beachfront homes: $250,000–$580,000 in San Juan del Sur
  • Condos: $170,000–$500,000 on the Emerald Coast
  • Colonial homes in Granada: Fully restored, under $200,000

Compare those numbers to equivalent properties across the border in Costa Rica, and the math speaks for itself. The Rivas coastal corridor (covering San Juan del Sur, Tola, and Popoyo) is appreciating at 7–10% annually, with five-year cumulative growth projected at 35–50%.

The Cost of Living Advantage

Nicaragua isn't just cheap to buy in — it's cheap to live in. A couple can live comfortably on $1,500 per month. Here's the breakdown:

  • Rent: $600–$1,200/month for a furnished 2-bedroom in San Juan del Sur
  • Local meals: $2–$4
  • Utilities: $50–$120/month
  • Overall cost of living: roughly 50% lower than the US

Inflation was just 2.7% in 2025, making Nicaragua one of the most price-stable economies in the region.

The Surf Tourism Engine

What's driving demand? Surf tourism. Nicaragua's Pacific coast has some of the best and most consistent waves in Central America, and the infrastructure is finally catching up to the surf.

The new Coastal Highway cut Managua-to-Popoyo travel time by over 30%, driving record tourism numbers and property transactions. The Tola/Popoyo corridor is seeing 12% annual growth in both property values and short-term rental demand.

San Juan del Sur's Airbnb market tells the story: 641 active listings generating an average of $15,537 in annual revenue per property, with an average daily rate of $178. Occupancy sits at 31% — lower than Costa Rica, but that's exactly the opportunity. As tourism infrastructure improves, occupancy rates will climb and yield more.

Can Foreigners Actually Own Property?

Yes. Nicaragua's constitution guarantees foreign property ownership rights — you have the same rights as a Nicaraguan national. No residency required, no minimum investment.

The key details:

  • Foreigners can own property outright in their own name
  • Within 5km of borders or coastlines, foreigners typically use 99-year leases instead of freehold title (this is standard and well-established)
  • Deeds must be notarized in Spanish
  • Title insurance is strongly recommended due to complex historical land records
  • New anti-money laundering regulations require verification of fund origins

The Four Zones to Watch

San Juan del Sur: The established expat hub. Restaurants, bars, yoga studios, and a growing international community. The "safest" play with the most liquidity.

Popoyo: Surf culture central, fastest appreciation in the country. More raw, less developed — but that's where the upside lives. Think Nosara, Costa Rica circa 2012.

Emerald Coast / Tola corridor: Higher-end developments, resort-style living. Mukul and Guacalito de la Isla set the luxury benchmark.

Granada: Colonial city, lower prices, more cultural than coastal. Beautiful restored homes under $200K. Different buyer profile — retirees and culture-seekers rather than surfers.

The Bottom Line

Nicaragua is not Costa Rica. The infrastructure is less developed, the tourism numbers are smaller, and the political landscape adds a layer of risk. But that's exactly why the prices are 40–60% lower. For buyers with a 5–10 year horizon who can tolerate some emerging-market friction, San Juan del Sur and the surrounding coast offer the kind of entry prices that simply don't exist anywhere else in Central America.

The window is narrowing. At 7–10% annual appreciation, today's $100K lot is a $150K lot in five years.
