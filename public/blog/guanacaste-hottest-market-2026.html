Costa Rica

Why Guanacaste Is the Hottest Property Market in Central America Right Now

By Sofia Mendez April 7, 2026 8 min read

If you've been paying attention to Central American real estate, you already know that Costa Rica is the region's most mature market. But within Costa Rica, one province is pulling away from the pack: Guanacaste. And the numbers tell a story that's hard to ignore.

The Numbers Don't Lie

Guanacaste's average apartment price sits around $2,990 per square meter, with houses averaging $1,582/sqm. But those are province-wide averages. In premium coastal towns like Tamarindo and Nosara, condos and villas command $3,000–$5,500/sqm. Ultra-luxury developments on the Papagayo Peninsula and Reserva Conchal push into the $8,000–$15,000/sqm range.

After a market correction of roughly 31% from 2024 peaks, analysts are forecasting 6–8% appreciation through 2026 — making this a compelling entry point for buyers who missed the last cycle.

Rental Yields That Beat Most Global Markets

This is where Guanacaste really shines. Long-term rental yields run 5.5–7.5% gross, which is solid. But short-term vacation rentals in tourist zones are delivering 9–11% gross yields.

The standout markets:

  • Tamarindo: 7–10% yields, with premium beachfront units hitting 12–17%. Q1 2026 data shows 29% more demand against 8% less inventory — a classic squeeze.
  • Nosara: 7–9% yields with nightly rates above $350 and occupancy around 45–51%. Q1 2026 saw 35% more bookings with 12% fewer listings.
  • Digital nomad-optimized units: Properties designed for remote workers are hitting 8–12% yields with 70–80% occupancy year-round.

These aren't theoretical projections. These are actual Q1 2026 numbers from property managers and booking platforms operating in the region.

The Airport Effect

Perhaps the single biggest driver of Guanacaste's real estate boom is Daniel Oduber Quiros International Airport (LIR) in Liberia. The airport handled 1.91 million passengers in 2024, up 16% year-over-year. And it surged another 16.3% in early 2026, welcoming over 230,000 international guests in just 60 days.

LIR now operates 26 direct international routes across 12 airlines, with top feeder markets including Toronto, Los Angeles, and Miami. A terminal expansion completing in late 2025/early 2026 is adding capacity to handle even more traffic.

And the momentum is accelerating. According to the Tico Times, LIR handled 793,075 passengers in Q1 2026 — a 12% year-over-year jump and the single busiest quarter in the airport's history. Canadian visitors alone surged 27%, now representing 26% of all international arrivals, with six direct Canadian connections operating year-round (Toronto, Montreal, Ottawa, Winnipeg, Vancouver, and Calgary). That's not a blip — it's a structural shift in who's discovering Guanacaste, and Canadian buyers have historically been among the most active purchasers in the coastal property market. More direct routes from wealthy North American cities means a deeper, more diversified pool of renters and buyers — the exact formula that drove up values in Puerto Vallarta and Playa del Carmen a decade ago.

More flights = more tourists = more rental demand = higher yields. It's a straightforward equation, and Guanacaste is on the right side of it.

Infrastructure Is Catching Up

The Costa Rican government and private sector are pouring money into Guanacaste's infrastructure:

  • Route 21 highway: A major highway tender expected in early 2026 will improve connectivity along the coast.
  • Nimboyores aqueduct: A multi-phase water infrastructure project running through 2030, solving one of the region's biggest development constraints.
  • Ciudad Nya: A 900-hectare, $1.2 billion mixed-use sustainable city near LIR airport — the largest development project in Central American history.

What About Foreign Ownership?

This is one of Costa Rica's biggest advantages. Foreigners can own titled property 100% in their own name — no special permits, no local partner required. The only exception is the Maritime Zone, which restricts ownership within 200 meters of the high-tide line for non-residents under 5 years. For a full breakdown of the legal process, title structures, and residency options, see our complete guide to buying property in Costa Rica as a foreigner.

Closing costs run 4–7% of the purchase price, including a 1.5% transfer tax. Annual property taxes are capped at just 0.25% of registered value — among the lowest in the hemisphere.

The Bottom Line

Guanacaste in 2026 offers a rare combination: a post-correction entry point, institutional-grade rental yields, a booming airport, and massive infrastructure investment. It's not the cheapest market in Central America (Nicaragua holds that title, with beachfront lots still starting under $100K), but it's arguably the most mature and lowest-risk.

If you're looking for a property investment in Latin America that offers both lifestyle appeal and strong financial returns, Guanacaste deserves a serious look. For buyers weighing multiple markets, our analysis of Buenos Aires as Latin America's best-value urban market in 2026 offers a useful contrast — high-yield city living versus coastal resort returns.
