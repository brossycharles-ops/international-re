Cost of Living in Costa Rica 2026: The Real Numbers

By International RE April 29, 2026 12 min read

Costa Rica is the most popular expat retirement destination in the Americas — and the most expensive country in Central America by a wide margin. The Pensionado visa requires $1,000/month income. The Rentista visa requires $2,500/month. Those thresholds exist for a reason: Costa Rica is not cheap.

That said, it delivers real value for the price: stable democracy, excellent private healthcare, high-quality produce, paved roads, reliable electricity, and one of the world's most biodiverse environments. The question isn't whether it's cheap — it isn't — but whether your budget buys the lifestyle you want.

This guide breaks down real 2026 costs by region, category, and lifestyle tier.

Currency note: Costa Rica uses the colón (CRC). 1 USD ≈ 510–530 CRC in 2026. Most expat-facing rents and real estate are quoted in USD. Local services, groceries, and restaurants are quoted in colones.

Rent by Region

Where you live in Costa Rica has an enormous impact on cost. Beach communities are 50–100% more expensive than equivalent quality in the Central Valley.

Escazú / Santa Ana (San José)

$700–1,400/mo

Upscale San José suburbs. Best infrastructure, closest to international airport, large expat community. Multiplaza malls, hospitals, good roads. Most popular with working expats and retirees who want city conveniences.

Heredia / Alajuela (Central Valley)

$500–900/mo

More affordable Central Valley towns 20–40 min from San José. Cooler climate at altitude, quieter neighborhoods, strong local infrastructure. Best value for budget-conscious expats who don't need beach access.

Tamarindo / Flamingo (Guanacaste)

$1,000–2,200/mo

North Pacific beach expat hub. Dry season climate (Nov–Apr). High tourist density, good English-speaking services, strong rental market. Most expensive beach region outside of premium Papagayo resort zone.

Nosara / Sámara

$900–1,800/mo

Quieter surf and wellness community. Yoga studios, organic cafés, strong digital nomad presence. Less tourist infrastructure than Tamarindo. Dirt roads in wet season are a lifestyle consideration.

Manuel Antonio / Quepos

$900–1,900/mo

South Pacific rainforest and beach. Year-round green season. Stunning national park. Smaller expat community than Guanacaste, higher humidity, but arguably better natural beauty and wildlife access.

Puerto Viejo (Caribbean)

$500–1,000/mo

Cheapest beach option in Costa Rica. Reggae-Caribbean culture, Afro-Caribbean cuisine, Cahuita National Park. Rainy most of the year. More rough-edged infrastructure. Budget expat community.

The feria (farmers market) is the biggest cost-saving tool in Costa Rica. Every town has a weekly Saturday market selling fresh tropical fruits, vegetables, eggs, and local products at 50–70% below supermarket prices. A family of two can eat extremely well on $150–200/month in fresh produce shopping exclusively at the feria and cooking most meals.

Imported goods are expensive. A bottle of Chilean wine at a supermarket costs $12–18. Imported cheese, deli meats, olive oil, and US cereals run 2–4x what you'd pay in the US. Costa Rica's import tariffs are high. Stick to local products and your grocery bill drops dramatically.

Healthcare

Costa Rica's healthcare is the primary reason most retirees choose it over other LatAm destinations. The two-tier system gives residents access to both public (CAJA) and private care:

CAJA enrollment (public system, monthly contribution)~7–11% of declared income (min ~$80/month for retirees)

Permanent residents who enroll in the CAJA get access to the full Costa Rican public healthcare system — the same system Ticos use — for a monthly contribution of roughly 7–11% of declared income. Wait times for non-emergency care can be long (months for specialists), so most expats maintain a private insurance plan for faster access alongside CAJA for major events.

Car ownership is close to mandatory outside San José. Public transport doesn't serve beach communities well, and roads to some areas require a 4WD in wet season. Budget $400–700/month for a car if you plan to live outside the capital — ownership, fuel, insurance, and maintenance combined.

FAQ: Cost of Living in Costa Rica

How much does it cost to live in Costa Rica per month?

$1,800–2,500/month for comfortable single-person living in the Central Valley. Budget is possible from $1,500/month. Beach living runs $3,000–5,500/month. Couples typically need $2,500–3,500/month comfortably. Costa Rica is the most expensive country in Central America.

How much is rent in Costa Rica in 2026?

Central Valley (Escazú/Santa Ana): furnished 1BR $700–1,400/month. Heredia/Alajuela: $500–900/month. Tamarindo: $1,000–2,200/month. Manuel Antonio: $900–1,900/month. Beach areas run 50–100% more than equivalent quality inland.

Is Costa Rica expensive to live in?

Yes, relative to the rest of Latin America. It's comparable to Southern Europe or Portugal. Significantly more expensive than Colombia, Argentina, or Mexico. Local produce, healthcare, and domestic services are affordable; imported goods, restaurants, and beach real estate are expensive.

What income do you need for the Pensionado visa?

$1,000/month in proven pension or Social Security income. The Rentista visa requires $2,500/month in passive income. The Digital Nomad visa requires $3,000/month in remote employment income. The Inversionista visa requires $150,000 invested in Costa Rica.
