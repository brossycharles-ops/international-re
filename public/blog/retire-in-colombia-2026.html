Retire in Colombia 2026: Visa, Cost of Living & Best Cities

April 29, 2026 · 12 min read · By International RE Research

Colombia has quietly become one of the world's top retirement destinations. The combination of a $750/month Pensionado visa threshold (the lowest in Latin America), year-round spring weather in Medellín, world-class coffee, and a healthcare system that's both excellent and affordable has attracted a rapidly growing wave of North American and European retirees.

This guide covers everything you need to know: visa requirements, the best cities, real monthly budgets, and what retirees in Colombia actually experience in 2026.

Colombia's key advantage over Panama and Costa Rica: The Pensionado visa income threshold is ~$750/month vs. $1,000/month in Costa Rica and ~$1,000/month in Panama. Colombia's cost of living is also 25–35% lower than Panama City. For retirees on modest fixed incomes, Colombia is the most accessible option in the region.

The Colombia Pensionado Visa: What You Need

Colombia's Pensionado (retirement) visa is a dedicated residency pathway for foreign retirees receiving a regular pension or retirement income. It's one of the simplest residency visas in Latin America.

RequirementDetails (2026)
Minimum monthly income~$750 USD/month (3× Colombian minimum wage)
Income sourcePension, Social Security, annuity, or retirement fund
Visa validity3 years, renewable

Where to applyOnline at cancilleria.gov.co or Colombian consulate

Social Security qualifies: US Social Security income is explicitly accepted as qualifying income for the Pensionado visa. A retiree receiving $1,500/month SS income comfortably exceeds the threshold. The income must be documented with bank statements and an official letter from the pension provider, apostilled for international use.

Step-by-Step Application Process

  1. Gather documentation: Passport (6+ months validity), bank statements (3–6 months), official pension letter (apostilled), background check from your home country (apostilled), passport photos.

  2. Apostille documents: All foreign documents must be apostilled by the issuing country's designated authority. For the US, state-level apostilles work for most documents; SSA letters require federal apostille.

  3. Submit online: Colombia's visa system (cancilleria.gov.co) accepts online applications with document uploads. No Colombian consulate visit required in most cases.

  4. Pay the fee: ~$270 USD via international credit card on the government portal.

  5. Receive visa: 15–30 business days. Stamped in your passport digitally — you receive a PDF visa to print and carry.

  6. Register with Migración Colombia: Within 15 days of arrival, register at any Migración Colombia office to receive your Cédula de Extranjería (foreign ID card).

Best Cities for Retirees in Colombia

Eternal Spring

Medellín

$1,200–2,200/mo

72°F year-round, large expat community in El Poblado and Laureles, excellent private hospitals (Clínica El Rosario, Clínica Las Américas), metro system, world-class restaurants. The undisputed top pick for most retirees.

Caribbean Beach

Cartagena

$1,400–2,800/mo

UNESCO-heritage walled city, Caribbean beaches, warm year-round. Pricier than Medellín due to tourism premium. Best for those who prioritize beach access and colonial architecture over cost savings.

Beach + Nature

Santa Marta

$1,000–1,800/mo

Fastest-growing expat destination on Colombia's coast. Sierra Nevada mountains meet Caribbean sea. Cheaper than Cartagena, near Tayrona National Park. Infrastructure still developing.

Capital City

Bogotá

$1,400–2,600/mo

World-class museums, 8 million people, altitude at 8,600 ft (cool climate). Best private hospitals in Colombia (Fundación Santa Fe). Suits retirees who want a major global city experience.

Coffee Region

Manizales / Armenia

$800–1,500/mo

Emerging ultra-budget options in the Coffee Region. Mild climate, colonial towns, coffee farm culture. Smaller expat communities but extremely affordable — under $1,000/month for singles is realistic.

Pacific Coast

Cali

$900–1,600/mo

World capital of salsa, warm climate, lower costs than Medellín. Less infrastructure for expats. Best for adventurous retirees who want authentic Colombia without a large English-speaking community.

Healthcare for Retirees in Colombia

Colombia's private healthcare is excellent, affordable, and accessible to foreign retirees. Legal residents must enroll in either the contributory EPS system (based on declared income) or pay voluntarily into Sisbén (subsidized system). Most retirees enroll in EPS and add a private prepaga top-up plan.

EPS enrollment tip: As a Pensionado visa holder, you declare your monthly pension income to the EPS and pay a contribution percentage (~12.5%) on that amount. A $1,500/month SS income → ~$190/month EPS contribution. This covers you comprehensively within the EPS network. Most retirees find this plus a prepaga top-up is all they need.

How much money do you need to retire in Colombia?

Colombia's Pensionado visa requires ~$750/month income. A comfortable single-person retirement in Medellín runs $1,200–1,800/month. A couple can live very comfortably for $1,800–2,800/month including rent, food, healthcare, and entertainment. Budget retirees in smaller cities (Manizales, Armenia) can live well on $800–1,200/month.

What is the Colombia Pensionado visa?

The Pensionado visa is Colombia's retirement residency visa, requiring approximately $750/month in pension income (3× the Colombian minimum wage). It's valid for 3 years, renewable, and leads to permanent residency after 5 continuous years. US Social Security explicitly qualifies as proof of income. Application is online at cancilleria.gov.co with an ~$270 fee.

What is the best city to retire in Colombia?

Medellín is the top choice for most retirees — 72°F year-round, large English-speaking expat community in El Poblado and Laureles, excellent private hospitals, and costs 25–35% lower than Panama City. Cartagena suits beach lovers. Santa Marta offers the best beach-to-cost ratio on the coast. Bogotá works for city-life seekers. The Coffee Region (Manizales) offers ultra-budget living under $1,000/month.

Is Colombia safe for retirees in 2026?

Medellín's expat neighborhoods (El Poblado, Laureles, Envigado) have excellent safety records and a mature expat infrastructure. Cartagena's Getsemaní and historic center are very safe. Standard urban precautions apply — use Cabify/InDriver over street taxis at night, avoid displaying valuables, stay in established expat neighborhoods. Colombia's major cities are considerably safer than their reputation from 20 years ago suggests.
