Cost of Living in Mexico City 2026: Real Numbers for Expats

From budget backpackers to remote-work professionals — what CDMX actually costs in 2026, neighborhood by neighborhood.

By International RE  ·  April 29, 2026  ·  10 min read

Bottom line: A comfortable expat life in Mexico City costs $1,800–$2,500/month for a single person in a good central neighborhood. Couples can live well on $2,500–$3,500/month. Gentrification has pushed rents up significantly since 2021, but CDMX still delivers exceptional value for its world-class food, culture, and infrastructure.

Rent by Neighborhood: Where Expats Actually Live

CDMX has 16 boroughs (alcaldías) and dozens of colonias. Expats concentrate in a handful of central neighborhoods with good walkability, safety, and amenities. Here's what you'll actually pay in 2026.

What CDMX Costs That Other Cities Don't

Altitude sickness (first 2–4 weeks): At 7,350 ft, many expats experience headaches, fatigue, and shortness of breath for the first week or two. Budget for a slower adjustment period and possibly a doctor visit if symptoms persist.

Air quality: CDMX has improved dramatically but still has pollution spikes (contingencias ambientales) during dry season (March–May). If you have respiratory issues, factor in air purifiers (~$80–$150 upfront) and replacement filters ($20–$40 every 3–6 months).

Water: Tap water is not safe to drink. Budget $10–$20/month for a garrafón (large water jug delivery service) — most buildings offer this, or use a Berkey/similar filtration system ($200–$300 one-time).

Visas: Can You Even Live There Long-Term?

Mexico allows most nationalities to enter visa-free for 180 days as a tourist. After that, your options are:

  • Residente Temporal: Requires ~$2,600/month income (or ~$43,000 in savings). Valid 1 year, renewable up to 4 years.
  • Residente Permanente: Requires ~$3,500/month income or ~$175,000 in savings. No renewal required.
  • Border runs: Technically possible but increasingly scrutinized. INM officers have been denying re-entry to obvious "border hoppers" — especially common at CDMX airport.

The remote-worker community has grown so large that Mexican authorities are paying more attention. If you plan to stay long-term, get the residency sorted properly.

Is Mexico City Right for You?

CDMX is ideal if you: want world-class culture, food, and nightlife on a mid-range budget; work remotely in a US or European time zone (UTC-6); want excellent healthcare without insurance premiums; speak or want to learn Spanish; thrive in a dense, urban environment.

Consider elsewhere if you: want a beach or nature lifestyle; have serious respiratory issues; need fast, guaranteed fiber internet for video work; prioritize a small, tight-knit expat community; want a car-friendly, suburban setup.
