Best Neighborhoods in Panama City for Property Buyers 2026

By International RE  ·  April 30, 2026  ·  13 min read

Panama City is the only major Latin American capital that runs entirely on US dollars, has a functioning land title system, and posts rental transaction volumes at 5-year highs. The question for buyers in 2026 isn't whether to buy — it's where.

The city's neighborhoods are radically different from each other. Casco Viejo is a UNESCO World Heritage colonial district with 9–13% Airbnb yields and chronic renovation risk. San Francisco is where multinationals house their staff and where long-term rental demand is most reliable. Costa del Este is Panama's version of Brickell — gleaming towers, corporate tenants, lower yields but higher price stability. Each makes sense for a different buyer. This guide breaks down the eight neighborhoods that matter, with real numbers.

Why Panama City First: The Macro Case

Panama processes 12,000+ real estate transactions per year — more than any other Central American country combined. Transaction volume is at a 5-year high in 2026. The dollar eliminates FX risk. Foreign buyers have identical ownership rights to locals — no trust structures, no restrictions. Rental demand is driven by 1,400+ multinationals with regional HQs in Panama City, creating a corporate tenant base that's unusually stable compared to tourism-dependent markets.

Panama's original city center, declared a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1997. Cobblestone streets, 17th–19th century Spanish colonial architecture, boutique hotels, and the fastest-gentrifying zip code in Central America. New supply is structurally impossible — every unit is a renovation of existing stock. This scarcity, combined with strong tourism and the neighborhood's growing dining and bar scene, drives the highest short-term rental yields in the city.

Strengths

  • Highest Airbnb yield in the city
  • UNESCO scarcity — no new supply competition
  • Strong capital appreciation history
  • Walking distance to banking and government districts

Considerations

  • Renovation costs are high ($400–$800/m²)
  • Building condition varies dramatically — due diligence is critical
  • Some blocks still transitional (safety varies by street)
  • Active STR management required (not set-and-forget)

San Francisco is Panama City's most liveable middle-upper neighborhood — walkable, safe, restaurant-dense, and home to a large share of the city's multinational workforce. The Parque Omar area and Via Brasil corridor attract corporate tenants on 12–24 month leases, producing stable income without the management intensity of short-term rentals. This is the neighborhood most international buyers end up in after visiting — it simply works.

Strengths

  • Highest volume of corporate long-term tenants
  • Walkable, excellent restaurants and services
  • Stable vacancy rates (often under 5%)
  • Good resale liquidity

Considerations

  • More expensive than El Cangrejo for similar quality
  • Traffic congestion during rush hour
  • Fewer distressed/value-add opportunities

El Cangrejo is the commercial and dining heart of Panama City — dense, gritty, and underpriced relative to San Francisco. Marbella, directly adjacent, is slightly more upscale. Together they offer the best value-for-yield equation in the city: lower entry prices than San Francisco, with comparable rental demand from the same corporate tenant pool. The new metro line passing through the area has pushed values up 10–15% since 2023.

Strengths

  • Lower entry price than San Francisco
  • Metro access boosts tenant demand
  • Diverse tenant mix (corporate + local professionals)
  • Strong yield relative to price

Considerations

  • Older building stock — inspect carefully
  • Less prestigious than Punta Pacífica for premium rents
  • Some buildings have deferred maintenance issues

Punta Pacífica is where the Trump Ocean Club towers, the JW Marriott, and the most expensive residential real estate in Panama sit — a waterfront peninsula with unobstructed Bay of Panama views. Yields are lower than elsewhere, but so is vacancy: senior executives from multinationals, embassy staff, and affluent Panamanians are the tenant pool. Best for buyers who want low management intensity, a reliable high-end tenant, and a trophy asset.

Strengths

  • Premium tenant quality — executives, diplomats
  • Very low vacancy in the $3,000+/mo segment
  • Waterfront views and branded building amenities
  • Easiest to manage (long leases, stable tenants)

Considerations

  • Lowest yield of any zone listed here
  • High HOA fees ($400–$800/mo) compress net returns
  • Entry prices are highest in the city

Costa del Este is Panama's newest planned business district — glass towers, wide streets, corporate campuses, and multinational offices. HSBC, Dell, 3M, and hundreds of regional HQs are clustered here. If you want a modern, air-conditioned, car-dependent lifestyle (similar to a US suburban business park), Costa del Este works well. Tenant demand from corporate relocations is high and consistent. Less character than San Francisco or Casco Viejo, but very businesslike yields.

Strengths

  • Modern buildings with low maintenance issues
  • Consistent year-over-year demand from MNC relocations
  • International school nearby (good for families)

Considerations

  • Car-dependent — not walkable
  • Less upside from gentrification (already developed)
  • High supply of new units can pressure rents

Bella Vista sits along the Via España corridor — Panama City's most commercially active street. The lowest entry prices in the city core. Heavy foot traffic and proximity to Casco Viejo. The old US Canal Zone — wide streets, greenery, low density, and a strong expat community. Home to several international schools and the City of Knowledge (a technology campus). The Buying Process in Panama: What Foreigners Need to Know

Panama has no restrictions on foreign property ownership. The process from offer to title transfer takes 45–90 days and follows a clear sequence:

  1. Letter of intent / offer: Signed by buyer and seller. Usually includes a 10% deposit held in escrow.
  2. Due diligence: Title search at the Registro Público (Public Registry) — verifies no liens, encumbrances, or title defects. Your attorney handles this.
  3. Promise to purchase: Binding contract signed before a Panamanian notary. The remaining balance (typically 90%) is paid at this stage or at closing.
  4. Title transfer: Registered at the Registro Público. Once registered, you have full legal title. Closing costs run 3–5% of purchase price (transfer tax, legal fees, registration).

Financing: Panamanian banks offer mortgages to foreign buyers at 6–8% interest for up to 70% LTV. Most international investors pay cash to avoid the bureaucracy and cost.

Our 2026 Neighborhood Picks

Best yield: Casco Viejo, for buyers who want to run a boutique short-term rental and are willing to navigate renovation complexity. Entry costs are high but the UNESCO supply ceiling makes appreciation reliable.

Best risk-adjusted return: San Francisco or El Cangrejo. Corporate tenant base, reliable long-term demand, manageable entry prices. El Cangrejo offers slightly better yield for buyers willing to buy older stock and accept lower prestige.

Best for passive investors: Costa del Este or Punta Pacífica. Modern buildings, high-quality tenants who sign long leases and rarely cause problems, lower management intensity. The trade-off is lower yield.

Best for owner-occupants: San Francisco or Clayton. If you're planning to live in Panama and rent elsewhere — or retire here — these two neighborhoods offer the best combination of livability and rental upside when you're not in residence.
