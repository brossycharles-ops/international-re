Expat Health Insurance in Latin America 2026: Country-by-Country Guide

April 29, 2026 · 12 min read · By International RE Research

Healthcare is the question every Latin America move eventually comes down to: what happens if something goes wrong? The good news is that private healthcare across most of Latin America is excellent and dramatically cheaper than the US. The bad news is that your US insurance almost certainly doesn't work here, and figuring out what to buy is genuinely confusing.

This guide breaks down the real options in each major expat destination — what local plans cost, when international insurance is worth it, and what most expats actually do.

The most important thing to know upfront: Latin America's private healthcare quality is genuinely high. Bogotá, Panama City, Mexico City, and Buenos Aires all have JCI-accredited hospitals staffed by US/European-trained specialists. You are not trading down on care quality — you are trading down on price, dramatically.

Do not rely on US domestic insurance abroad. ACA plans, employer plans, and especially Medicare do not cover routine care outside the US. At minimum, purchase medical evacuation coverage before you leave — a helicopter transfer from a rural area can exceed $50,000.

🇵🇦 Panama RECOMMENDED

Panama uses a USD economy and has among the best private hospitals in Central America — including branches of Johns Hopkins-affiliated facilities. The public Caja de Seguro Social (CSS) is technically available to legal residents but has long waits. Most expats use private insurance from day one.

🇨🇴 Colombia RECOMMENDED

Colombia has a mandatory national health system (EPS) that legal residents must enroll in, paying roughly 12.5% of declared monthly income. EPS quality varies significantly — contributors can access the best public facilities, but wait times can be long. Most expats supplement with a prepaga (private) plan for faster access and broader coverage.

🇨🇷 Costa Rica PLAN REQUIRED

Costa Rica's CAJA (Caja Costarricense de Seguro Social) is mandatory for all legal residents — pensionados, rentistas, and workers. It's one of the better public systems in Latin America, but enrollment can be bureaucratically complex, and many expats add a private supplement for faster specialist access. Budget the CAJA contribution from day one of your residency application.

🇲🇽 Mexico RECOMMENDED

Mexico has very strong private healthcare infrastructure in Mexico City, Guadalajara, Monterrey, and major expat areas (Puerto Vallarta, San Miguel). Private care without insurance is often so affordable that many expats simply pay out-of-pocket for routine visits and buy international insurance for major events only. IMSS enrollment is available to legal residents for a modest annual fee.

🇺🇾 Uruguay STABLE

Uruguay has a mandatory FONASA health system for legal residents, with contributions based on income. Quality is consistently high across both the public ASSE network and the private mutualistas. Many expats find FONASA + a top mutualista is all they need — Uruguay punches above its weight in healthcare reliability.

Medical Evacuation: The Non-Negotiable

Whatever else you decide, do not leave home without medical evacuation coverage. A helicopter or air ambulance transfer — from a coastal property, a mountain area, or any location without a top hospital — costs $30,000–$80,000 out of pocket. Most comprehensive international plans include this. If you go local-only, add a standalone evacuation policy from MEDJET or Global Rescue ($300–500/year).

What is the best health insurance for expats in Latin America?

For most long-term expats, the best approach is a local private plan (EPS in Colombia, prepaga in Argentina, private insurance in Panama) for routine care, combined with an international policy from Cigna Global or Allianz for major medical events and US access. Local-only is fine if you're settled in one country; international-only is overkill and overpriced for day-to-day care.

Do I need international health insurance if I live in Latin America?

Not necessarily. Many expats in Colombia, Argentina, and Uruguay rely entirely on local plans, which cover excellent quality care for $60–220/month. International coverage makes most sense if you travel frequently between countries, need US access, or are in an adventure/remote-travel lifestyle. At minimum, buy medical evacuation coverage — it's $300–500/year and protects against the single biggest financial risk.

How much does health insurance cost for expats in Latin America?

Local plans: $60–380/month depending on country and tier. International plans: $150–500/month for a 45-year-old non-smoker. A typical comfortable expat setup — local plan plus international backup — runs $200–500/month total, compared to $600–1,500/month for equivalent US domestic coverage.

Can I use US health insurance in Latin America?

Almost certainly not for routine care. Most US domestic health plans (ACA, employer) cover only emergency stabilization outside the US, and Medicare covers nothing internationally. If you move abroad, you need a local plan, an international expat policy, or both. Verify with your current insurer before departing — do not assume you're covered.
