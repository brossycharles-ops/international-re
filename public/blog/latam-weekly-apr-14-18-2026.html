Weekly Roundup

Latin America Real Estate Weekly — April 14–18, 2026

By The Editorial Team April 18, 2026 5 min read

Four markets, one week. Here's what moved across Costa Rica, Nicaragua, Argentina, and Chile — and what it means for investors watching the region.

Costa Rica: Residency Threshold Rises to $200K

Effective February 2026, Costa Rica's rentista and investor residency programs now require a minimum investment of USD $200,000 — up from the previous $150,000 floor. The change reflects the government's effort to align immigration benefits with rising property values, particularly along the Pacific coast.

The timing matters. Guanacaste property prices have climbed 8–12% annually in prime areas like Tamarindo, Nosara, and Papagayo, putting many entry-level listings already above the new threshold. For buyers who've been on the fence, the regulatory shift adds urgency: qualifying properties that checked both investment and lifestyle boxes last year are now fewer. A separate modernization of the Condominium Law (in force since January 2026) has streamlined phased development approvals, which is beginning to bring new mixed-use inventory to the market. Rental yields in Guanacaste vacation properties are holding at 8–12% annually.

Nicaragua: Coastal Demand Outpaces Supply in Tola and San Juan del Sur

Nicaragua's Pacific corridor continues its quiet ascent. Analysts tracking the Tola, Rivas, and Emerald Coast submarkets are projecting 7–12% price growth over the next 12 months, driven by constrained beachfront supply and rising interest from North American retirees and remote workers priced out of Costa Rica.

The national average property price sits around USD $105,000, and annual property taxes remain among the lowest in Central America at roughly 1% of cadastral value. San Juan del Sur — already known for its surf scene and expat-friendly infrastructure — is seeing luxury villa completions sell before construction finishes. For investors comfortable with frontier-market risk, the spread between current prices and regional comparables in Costa Rica or Panama remains compelling.

Argentina: Domestic Mortgages Return, Yields Hit 7.3%

Buenos Aires is entering what analysts are calling a "reawakening" phase. After nearly a decade of all-cash-only transactions, domestic mortgage lending has quietly returned since the lifting of capital controls in April 2025. The effect is beginning to show in the data: average gross rental yields across the city have climbed to 7.3%, with high-demand micro-markets in Villa Crespo, Almagro, and Chacarita hitting 8–9% gross on studios and one-bedrooms.

The repeal of the 2020 Rental Law — which had suppressed institutional landlord activity with rigid price controls — has restored freedom of contract, drawing more professional operators back into the market. City-wide prices appreciated 5–8% in USD terms over the past year, with a further 5% gain projected through end of 2026. Prime Buenos Aires neighborhoods still average $1,250–$2,300 per square meter, a fraction of comparable European or North American capitals.

Chile: Buyer's Market Deepens as Mortgage Rates Hit 4.1%

Chile's residential market remains firmly in buyers' territory. Nationwide inventory has exceeded 100,000 units, and typical purchase negotiations are landing at 3–7% below asking price — unusual for a market that ran hot through most of the early 2020s.

The week's headline number: mortgage rates have dropped to approximately 4.1%, the lowest level since 2021, opening the door for a new cohort of qualified buyers who had been sitting out. Santiago properties are projected to appreciate 3–7% in 2026 as the rate environment and cleared inventory combine to find a floor. The caveat: financing restrictions for non-residents remain tight, making this window primarily accessible to Chilean residents and cash buyers from abroad. For our deeper look at where in Chile to deploy capital right now, see our piece on Santiago's supply squeeze and the Santiago vs. Lake District breakdown.

The Bigger Picture

Zooming out, the Latin American real estate market is valued at USD $1.16 trillion in 2026, with analysts projecting it to nearly double by 2034 on the back of rapid urbanization, nearshoring demand, and growing middle-class homeownership. The structural tailwinds are real — but so are the on-the-ground frictions: bureaucratic property registration processes, currency volatility in some markets, and evolving legal frameworks require investors to stay current. That's what this weekly is for.

We'll be watching all four markets closely next week.
